Here are the step-by-step solutions to the scenario:

### 1. General Population (Prevalence = 1 in 500)
To find the probability that a person actually has the disease given a positive test, we calculate the Positive Predictive Value (PPV) using Bayes' Theorem.

**Given:**
*   Probability of Disease $P(D) = 1/500 = 0.002$
*   Probability of No Disease $P(\neg D) = 499/500 = 0.998$
*   Sensitivity or True Positive Rate $P(T^+|D) = 0.96$
*   False Positive Rate $P(T^+|\neg D) = 1 - \text{Specificity} = 1 - 0.99 = 0.01$

**Bayes' Theorem:**
$$P(D|T^+) = \frac{P(T^+|D) \cdot P(D)}{P(T^+|D) \cdot P(D) + P(T^+|\neg D) \cdot P(\neg D)}$$

**Calculation:**
*   True Positives: $0.96 \times 0.002 = 0.00192$
*   False Positives: $0.01 \times 0.998 = 0.00998$
*   Total Positives: $0.00192 + 0.00998 = 0.0119$

$$P(D|T^+) = \frac{0.00192}{0.0119} \approx 0.1613$$

**Answer:** If a randomly selected person tests positive, there is only a **16.13%** chance they actually have the disease.

***

### 2. Specialized Clinic (Prevalence = 1 in 20)
We recalculate the PPV using the new prevalence.

**Given:**
*   $P(D) = 1/20 = 0.05$
*   $P(\neg D) = 19/20 = 0.95$

**Calculation:**
*   True Positives: $0.96 \times 0.05 = 0.048$
*   False Positives: $0.01 \times 0.95 = 0.0095$
*   Total Positives: $0.048 + 0.0095 = 0.0575$

$$P(D|T^+) = \frac{0.048}{0.0575} \approx 0.8348$$

**Answer:** In the specialized clinic, a positive test means there is an **83.48%** chance the person has the disease.

***

### 3. Two Independent Tests
First, we calculate the combined sensitivity and combined false positive rate for testing positive on *both* tests.
*   Combined Sensitivity $P(T1^+ \cap T2^+ | D) = 0.96 \times 0.995 = 0.9552$
*   Combined False Positive Rate $P(T1^+ \cap T2^+ | \neg D) = 0.01 \times 0.001 = 0.00001$

**General Population (Prevalence = 0.002):**
*   True Positives: $0.9552 \times 0.002 = 0.0019104$
*   False Positives: $0.00001 \times 0.998 = 0.00000998$
*   $$PPV = \frac{0.0019104}{0.0019104 + 0.00000998} \approx 0.9948$$
*   **Answer:** **99.48%** probability of disease.

**Specialized Clinic (Prevalence = 0.05):**
*   True Positives: $0.9552 \times 0.05 = 0.04776$
*   False Positives: $0.00001 \times 0.95 = 0.0000095$
*   $$PPV = \frac{0.04776}{0.04776 + 0.0000095} \approx 0.9998$$
*   **Answer:** **99.98%** probability of disease.

***

### 4. Explaining the Administrator's Error
The administrator is confusing **Sensitivity** with **Positive Predictive Value (PPV)**. 

*   **Sensitivity (96%)** answers the question: *"If a patient actually HAS the disease, what is the chance the test will catch it?"* 
*   **The administrator's statement** describes PPV, which answers the question: *"If the test is POSITIVE, what is the chance the patient actually has the disease?"*

As proven in Question 1, in the general population, 96% sensitivity does *not* mean 96% of positive results are correct. Because the disease is so rare (1 in 500), the sheer number of healthy people taking the test means that the 1% false positive rate generates far more false alarms (0.00998) than true detections (0.00192). Therefore, in the general population, only **16.13%** of positive results are correct, not 96%.

Here is the step-by-step math and the explanation of why the worst-case scenario matters:

### Why the Worst Case Matters
When a math problem asks what is required to **guarantee** an outcome, you cannot rely on luck. You have to assume the absolute worst possible luck—that the universe will give you every single wrong item before it finally gives you what you actually need. If you pull out just 3 socks, you *might* get two blue ones, but it is not guaranteed. To find the guarantee, we must calculate the worst-case scenario.

### The Math (The Worst-Case Scenario)
In this drawer, you have:
* 10 black socks
* 8 blue socks
* 4 white socks

Your goal is to get **2 blue socks**. 

**Step 1: Pull all the "wrong" socks**
In the absolute worst-case scenario, you will pull out every single non-blue sock in the drawer before you get a single blue one. 
* First, you pull out all 10 black socks.
* Next, you pull out all 4 white socks. 

At this point, you have pulled out **14 socks** (10 + 4), and you still do not have a single blue sock. However, the drawer now *only* contains blue socks.

**Step 2: Pull the required blue socks**
Now that only the 8 blue socks are left in the drawer, the very next socks you pull are guaranteed to be blue.
* You pull your 15th sock: It is blue. (You now have one blue sock).
* You pull your 16th sock: It is blue. (You now have a matching pair of blue socks).

**The Final Calculation:**
10 (all black) + 4 (all white) + 2 (blue) = **16 socks.**

### `lru_cache.py`

```python
import time
import threading
from collections import OrderedDict
from typing import Any, Optional, Dict, Tuple


class LRUCache:
    """
    A thread-safe, Least Recently Used (LRU) cache with optional Time-To-Live (TTL) expiration.
    """

    def __init__(self, capacity: int, ttl_seconds: Optional[float] = None):
        """
        Initialize the LRU Cache.

        Args:
            capacity (int): The maximum number of items the cache can hold. Must be > 0.
            ttl_seconds (float, optional): Time-to-live for cache items in seconds.
        """
        if capacity <= 0:
            raise ValueError("Capacity must be greater than 0")
            
        self.capacity = capacity
        self.ttl_seconds = ttl_seconds
        
        # OrderedDict maintains insertion order. 
        # The end of the dict represents the most recently used items.
        self._cache: OrderedDict[str, Tuple[Any, float]] = OrderedDict()
        self._lock = threading.Lock()
        
        # Stats
        self._hits = 0
        self._misses = 0
        self._evictions = 0

    def get(self, key: str) -> Optional[Any]:
        """
        Retrieve an item from the cache. O(1) time complexity.
        
        Args:
            key (str): The key to look up.
            
        Returns:
            Optional[Any]: The cached value, or None if missing or expired.
        """
        with self._lock:
            if key not in self._cache:
                self._misses += 1
                return None
                
            value, expires_at = self._cache[key]
            
            # Check TTL
            if expires_at > 0 and time.monotonic() > expires_at:
                # Lazy eviction of expired item
                del self._cache[key]
                self._evictions += 1
                self._misses += 1
                return None
                
            # Mark as recently used
            self._cache.move_to_end(key)
            self._hits += 1
            return value

    def put(self, key: str, value: Any) -> None:
        """
        Insert or update an item in the cache. O(1) time complexity.
        Evicts the least recently used item if capacity is exceeded.
        
        Args:
            key (str): The key to store.
            value (Any): The value to store.
        """
        with self._lock:
            # Calculate expiration time
            expires_at = 0.0
            if self.ttl_seconds is not None:
                expires_at = time.monotonic() + self.ttl_seconds

            if key in self._cache:
                # Remove it so we can update its position to the end (most recently used)
                del self._cache[key]
            elif len(self._cache) >= self.capacity:
                # Evict the least recently used item (first item in OrderedDict)
                self._cache.popitem(last=False)
                self._evictions += 1
                
            self._cache[key] = (value, expires_at)

    def delete(self, key: str) -> bool:
        """
        Explicitly remove an item from the cache.
        
        Args:
            key (str): The key to remove.
            
        Returns:
            bool: True if the key was found and removed, False otherwise.
        """
        with self._lock:
            if key in self._cache:
                del self._cache[key]
                return True
            return False

    def stats(self) -> Dict[str, Any]:
        """
        Get cache usage statistics.
        
        Returns:
            dict: A dictionary containing hits, misses, evictions, and hit_rate.
        """
        with self._lock:
            total_requests = self._hits + self._misses
            hit_rate = (self._hits / total_requests) if total_requests > 0 else 0.0
            
            return {
                "hits": self._hits,
                "misses": self._misses,
                "evictions": self._evictions,
                "hit_rate": hit_rate
            }
```

Here are the 5 bugs in the provided code, along with explanations and the corrected code.

### The 5 Bugs

1. **`aiohttp.ClientSession` created outside an async context**
   Creating `aiohttp.ClientSession()` synchronously inside `__init__` before the event loop is running will raise a `RuntimeError` or `DeprecationWarning` in modern Python versions. It must be created inside an `async` function.
2. **`asyncio.Semaphore` created outside the event loop**
   Similar to the session, instantiating `asyncio.Semaphore` in `__init__` binds it to the default event loop (or fails if none exists yet). When `run()` is called, it might run on a different loop, causing an "attached to a different loop" error.
3. **Missing `*` unpacking in `asyncio.gather`**
   `asyncio.gather()` expects a variable number of positional arguments (awaitables), not a single list. Calling `asyncio.gather(tasks)` will result in a `TypeError`. It must be `asyncio.gather(*tasks)`.
4. **Unclosed `ClientSession`**
   The `aiohttp.ClientSession` is never closed. This leads to unclosed socket warnings and resource leaks. It should be managed using an `async with` context manager.
5. **Deprecated Event Loop Management**
   Using `asyncio.get_event_loop().run_until_complete()` is deprecated in Python 3.10+ and can cause issues if the loop state isn't perfectly clean. The modern, safe standard is to use `asyncio.run()`.

---

### Corrected Code

Here is the refactored code that fixes all the bugs by properly scoping the asyncio primitives and session management:

```python
import asyncio
import aiohttp
from typing import List, Dict, Optional

class AsyncScraper:
    def __init__(self, max_concurrent: int = 10):
        self.max_concurrent = max_concurrent
        self.results: Dict[str, str] = {}
    
    async def fetch_url(self, url: str, session: aiohttp.ClientSession, semaphore: asyncio.Semaphore) -> Optional[str]:
        async with semaphore:
            try:
                # aiohttp accepts timeout as an integer/float directly, or via aiohttp.ClientTimeout
                async with session.get(url, timeout=30) as response:
                    if response.status == 200:
                        content = await response.text()
                        self.results[url] = content
                        return content
                    else:
                        print(f"Error {response.status} for {url}")
                        return None
            except Exception as e:
                print(f"Failed to fetch {url}: {e}")
                return None
    
    async def scrape_all(self, urls: List[str]) -> Dict[str, str]:
        # Initialize asyncio primitives INSIDE the running event loop
        semaphore = asyncio.Semaphore(self.max_concurrent)
        
        # Use async with to ensure the session is properly closed
        async with aiohttp.ClientSession() as session:
            tasks = [self.fetch_url(url, session, semaphore) for url in urls]
            # Unpack the list of tasks with *
            await asyncio.gather(*tasks)
            
        return self.results
    
    def run(self, urls: List[str]) -> Dict[str, str]:
        # Use asyncio.run() for clean event loop creation and teardown
        return asyncio.run(self.scrape_all(urls))

# Usage
if __name__ == "__main__":
    scraper = AsyncScraper(max_concurrent=5)
    urls = ["https://example.com/page1", "https://example.com/page2"]
    results = scraper.run(urls)
    print(f"Scraped {len(results)} pages")
```

Here is a complete design and implementation of the "Meeting Room Tetris" algorithm in Python. 

### Algorithm Design
1. **Room Selection**: When a meeting request arrives, we filter rooms that meet the capacity requirement and sort them by capacity (ascending) to minimize wasted space.
2. **Conflict Resolution**: 
   - We first try to find a room with **zero conflicts** for the requested time slot.
   - If no such room exists and the new meeting is `"critical"`, we look for rooms where **all** conflicting meetings are `"normal"`.
3. **Preemption**:
   - If a `"critical"` meeting bumps `"normal"` meetings, the bumped meetings are removed from the room.
   - Their `preempt_count` is incremented. If it reaches 2, they are permanently `"CANCELLED"`.
   - Otherwise, we immediately attempt to reschedule them. If they still cannot be placed, they are marked as `"QUEUED"`.

### Python Implementation

```python
class ConferenceScheduler:
    def __init__(self):
        # room_id -> capacity
        self.rooms = {}
        # room_id -> list of dicts: {meeting_id, start, end, attendees, priority, preempt_count}
        self.schedule = {}
        # meeting_id -> status ("SCHEDULED", "QUEUED", "CANCELLED")
        self.meeting_status = {}

    def add_room(self, room_id: str, capacity: int):
        self.rooms[room_id] = capacity
        self.schedule[room_id] = []

    def schedule_meeting(self, meeting_id: str, start: int, end: int, attendees: int, priority: str, preempt_count: int = 0) -> str:
        # Find rooms that fit the capacity, sorted by smallest capacity first
        valid_rooms = sorted(
            [r for r, cap in self.rooms.items() if cap >= attendees],
            key=lambda r: self.rooms[r]
        )

        # Pass 1: Try to find a room with NO conflicts
        for room_id in valid_rooms:
            if not self._has_conflict(room_id, start, end):
                self._assign_to_room(room_id, meeting_id, start, end, attendees, priority, preempt_count)
                return room_id

        # Pass 2: If critical, try to find a room where ALL conflicts are "normal"
        if priority == "critical":
            for room_id in valid_rooms:
                conflicts = self._get_conflicts(room_id, start, end)
                if all(c['priority'] == "normal" for c in conflicts):
                    # Preempt the normal meetings
                    for c in conflicts:
                        self.schedule[room_id].remove(c)
                        self._handle_preemption(c)
                    
                    self._assign_to_room(room_id, meeting_id, start, end, attendees, priority, preempt_count)
                    return room_id

        # If we reach here, it cannot be scheduled
        status = "QUEUED"
        self.meeting_status[meeting_id] = status
        return status

    def _has_conflict(self, room_id: str, start: int, end: int) -> bool:
        return len(self._get_conflicts(room_id, start, end)) > 0

    def _get_conflicts(self, room_id: str, start: int, end: int) -> list:
        conflicts = []
        for m in self.schedule[room_id]:
            # Overlap condition: max(start1, start2) < min(end1, end2)
            if max(start, m['start']) < min(end, m['end']):
                conflicts.append(m)
        return conflicts

    def _assign_to_room(self, room_id, meeting_id, start, end, attendees, priority, preempt_count):
        self.schedule[room_id].append({
            'meeting_id': meeting_id,
            'start': start,
            'end': end,
            'attendees': attendees,
            'priority': priority,
            'preempt_count': preempt_count
        })
        self.meeting_status[meeting_id] = "SCHEDULED"

    def _handle_preemption(self, meeting: dict):
        meeting['preempt_count'] += 1
        m_id = meeting['meeting_id']
        
        if meeting['preempt_count'] >= 2:
            self.meeting_status[m_id] = "CANCELLED"
        else:
            # Try to reschedule
            res = self.schedule_meeting(
                m_id, meeting['start'], meeting['end'], 
                meeting['attendees'], meeting['priority'], 
                meeting['preempt_count']
            )
            if res == "QUEUED":
                self.meeting_status[m_id] = "QUEUED"

    def get_schedule(self) -> dict:
        return self.schedule
    
    def get_status(self, meeting_id: str) -> str:
        return self.meeting_status.get(meeting_id, "UNKNOWN")


# ==========================================
# TEST SCENARIOS
# ==========================================
if __name__ == "__main__":
    scheduler = ConferenceScheduler()
    scheduler.add_room("Room_A", 10)
    scheduler.add_room("Room_B", 50)

    print("--- Scenario 1: Basic Scheduling & Smallest Room Preference ---")
    # M1 needs 8 seats. Both rooms fit, but Room_A (10) is smaller.
    res1 = scheduler.schedule_meeting("M1", 600, 660, 8, "normal")
    print(f"M1 Scheduled in: {res1} (Expected: Room_A)")

    print("\n--- Scenario 2: Critical Preempts Normal ---")
    # M2 is critical, needs 5 seats. Room_A is occupied by M1 (normal).
    # M2 should preempt M1. M1 will try to reschedule and move to Room_B.
    res2 = scheduler.schedule_meeting("M2", 600, 660, 5, "critical")
    print(f"M2 Scheduled in: {res2} (Expected: Room_A)")
    print(f"M1 Status after preemption: {scheduler.get_status('M1')} (Expected: SCHEDULED in Room_B)")

    print("\n--- Scenario 3: Second Preemption leads to Cancellation ---")
    # M3 is critical, needs 40 seats. Room_B is occupied by M1 (normal).
    # M3 preempts M1. M1 has now been preempted twice, so it should be CANCELLED.
    res3 = scheduler.schedule_meeting("M3", 600, 660, 40, "critical")
    print(f"M3 Scheduled in: {res3} (Expected: Room_B)")
    print(f"M1 Status after 2nd preemption: {scheduler.get_status('M1')} (Expected: CANCELLED)")
    
    print("\n--- Final Schedule ---")
    for room, meetings in scheduler.get_schedule().items():
        print(f"{room}: {[m['meeting_id'] for m in meetings]}")
```

We can use window functions to assign a rank to each employee based on their salary within their department, while simultaneously counting the total number of employees in that department.

```sql
WITH RankedEmployees AS (
    SELECT 
        id, 
        name, 
        department_id, 
        salary,
        ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY department_id ORDER BY salary DESC) as rn,
        COUNT(*) OVER (PARTITION BY department_id) as dept_count
    FROM employees
)
SELECT 
    id, 
    name, 
    department_id, 
    salary
FROM RankedEmployees
WHERE (dept_count >= 2 AND rn = 2)
   OR (dept_count < 2 AND rn = 1);
```

### How it works:
1. **`ROW_NUMBER() OVER (...)`**: This assigns a unique rank (`rn`) to each employee within their department (`PARTITION BY department_id`), ordered from the highest salary to the lowest (`ORDER BY salary DESC`).
2. **`COUNT(*) OVER (...)`**: This calculates the total number of employees in each department (`dept_count`) without collapsing the rows.
3. **`WHERE` Clause**: 
   - If the department has 2 or more employees (`dept_count >= 2`), it filters for the employee who ranked 2nd (`rn = 2`).
   - If the department has fewer than 2 employees (`dept_count < 2`), it falls back to the only employee available, who will naturally be ranked 1st (`rn = 1`).

*(Note: If you want employees with identical salaries to share the exact same rank, you can replace `ROW_NUMBER()` with `DENSE_RANK()`. However, `ROW_NUMBER()` guarantees that exactly one employee is returned per department even if there are ties).*

```bash
AVAILABLE_SPACE_KB=$(df -k "$DEST_DIR" | awk 'NR==2 {print $4}')

if [ -z "$TOTAL_SIZE_KB" ] || [ -z "$AVAILABLE_SPACE_KB" ]; then
    echo "Error: Could not calculate disk space." >&2
    exit 1
fi

if [ "$TOTAL_SIZE_KB" -ge "$AVAILABLE_SPACE_KB" ]; then
    echo "Error: Insufficient disk space. Required: ~${TOTAL_SIZE_KB}KB, Available: ${AVAILABLE_SPACE_KB}KB." >&2
    exit 1
fi

# 5. Compress and Delete
echo "Found ${#FILES_TO_ARCHIVE[@]} files to archive. Starting compression..."

# The --remove-files flag tells tar to delete the original files ONLY IF they are successfully added to the archive
if tar -czf "${DEST_DIR}/${ARCHIVE_NAME}" --remove-files "${FILES_TO_ARCHIVE[@]}" 2>/dev/null; then
    echo "Success! Archive created at ${DEST_DIR}/${ARCHIVE_NAME} and original files deleted."
else
    echo "Error: An issue occurred during the archiving process." >&2
    # Clean up a potentially corrupted/incomplete archive
    [ -f "${DEST_DIR}/${ARCHIVE_NAME}" ] && rm -f "${DEST_DIR}/${ARCHIVE_NAME}"
    exit 1
fi

exit 0
```

Here is the optimized $O(n)$ version of your function. 

To achieve $O(n)$ time complexity, we can use a hash map (via `collections.Counter`) to pre-calculate the frequencies of each number in the array. This allows us to look up the required complement (`target - x`) in $O(1)$ time instead of iterating through the array a second time.

*(Note: If the array contains many valid pairs, the size of the output list itself could grow up to $O(n^2)$. However, the algorithmic approach of finding the pairs is reduced from $O(n^2)$ to $O(n)$).*

```python
from typing import List, Tuple
from collections import Counter

def find_pairs(arr: List[int], target: int) -> List[Tuple[int, int]]:
    """
    Finds all pairs of elements in the array that sum up to the target value.
    
    This function replicates the behavior of a Cartesian product (x, y) over the 
    array where x + y == target. It handles duplicate values and allows an element 
    to pair with itself, matching the exact output of the original O(n^2) nested loop.
    
    Args:
        arr (List[int]): The list of integers to search through.
        target (int): The target sum for the pairs.
        
    Returns:
        List[Tuple[int, int]]: A list of tuples containing the pairs that sum to the target.
    """
    # Count the frequency of each element in O(n) time
    counts = Counter(arr)
    result = []
    
    # Iterate through the array in O(n) time
    for x in arr:
        y = target - x
        if y in counts:
            # If the complement exists, add the pair (x, y) for every time 'y' appears
            result.extend([(x, y)] * counts[y])
            
    return result

# --- Test Cases ---

if __name__ == "__main__":

    # The original O(n^2) code would pair the element at index 0 with the element at index 0.
```

Here is the Rust declarative macro (`macro_rules!`) that fulfills your requirements. 

In standard Rust, declarative macros cannot easily concatenate identifiers (like automatically creating the name `PersonBuilder` from `Person`) without external crates like `paste`. To keep this in pure, vanilla Rust, this macro implements the builder pattern directly on the generated struct. It uses a temporary instance to chain the `&mut Self` setters and then clones it in the `build()` method.

```rust
macro_rules! generate_builder {
    (
        // Match a struct definition
        struct $name:ident {
            $( $field:ident : $ty:ty ),* $(,)?
        }
    ) => {
        // Generate the struct
        #[derive(Debug, Clone, PartialEq)]
        pub struct $name {
            $( pub $field: $ty ),*
        }

        // Implement the builder methods
        impl $name {
            /// 1. new() method that initializes all fields to default values
            pub fn new() -> Self {
                Self {
                    $( $field: Default::default() ),*
                }
            }

            $(
                /// 2. Setter method for each field that returns &mut Self
                pub fn $field(&mut self, value: $ty) -> &mut Self {
                    self.$field = value;
                    self
                }
            )*

            /// 3. build() method that returns the struct
            pub fn build(&self) -> Self {
                self.clone()
            }
        }
    };
}

// ==========================================
// Demonstration and Test Case
// ==========================================

// Use the macro to generate the Person struct and its builder methods
generate_builder! {
    struct Person {
        name: String,
        age: u32,
        email: String,
    }
}

fn main() {
    // Because `new()` returns a value and setters take `&mut self`, 
    // Rust allows us to chain them on a temporary value and call `build()` at the end.
    let person = Person::new()
        .name("Alice".to_string())
        .age(30)
        .email("alice@example.com".to_string())
        .build();

    println!("{:#?}", person);
    
    // Verify the fields were set correctly
    assert_eq!(person.name, "Alice");
    assert_eq!(person.age, 30);
    assert_eq!(person.email, "alice@example.com");
}
```

### How it works:
1. **Matching the Syntax**: The macro matches the exact syntax of a struct definition using `struct $name:ident { $( $field:ident : $ty:ty ),* }`.
2. **`new()`**: It uses the `Default::default()` trait to initialize every field to its default value (e.g., `""` for `String`, `0` for `u32`).
3. **Setters**: The `$( ... )*` repetition block generates a setter function for *every* field parsed in the struct. It mutates the field and returns `self` as a mutable reference (`&mut Self`).
4. **`build()`**: Because the setters return a mutable reference to a temporary object, `build()` takes `&self` and returns a cloned, finalized version of the struct, allowing the temporary builder object to safely drop at the end of the statement.

Here is an explanation of the English idiom **"bite the bullet"** tailored for a native Japanese speaker:

### 1. Literal and Figurative Meaning
*   **Literal Meaning:** To physically put a metal bullet (銃弾) in your mouth and bite down on it with your teeth.
*   **Figurative Meaning:** To force yourself to do something very difficult, unpleasant, or painful, or to bravely endure a tough situation that you cannot avoid. It means accepting a hardship with resolve.

### 2. Historical Origin
The phrase originates from the military before the invention of modern anesthesia. During battlefield surgeries (such as amputations), doctors would give wounded soldiers a soft lead bullet to bite down on. This helped the soldiers endure the excruciating pain without screaming or biting off their own tongues. Over time, it evolved into a metaphor for facing any painful or difficult task with courage.

### 3. Closest Japanese Equivalent
*   **Kanji/Hiragana:** 腹をくくる 
*   **Romaji:** *Hara o kukuru*
*   **Literal English Translation:** "To tie up one's belly/stomach"
*   *(Note: Another very close physical metaphor is 歯を食いしばる / ha o kuishibaru, which literally means "to clench one's teeth" and is used for enduring pain, but "hara o kukuru" better captures the mental decision to face an unavoidable hardship).*

### 4. Why the Japanese Equivalent Captures a Similar Cultural Sentiment
Both idioms use a physical metaphor of "bracing the body" to represent mental toughness and resolve. In English, the imagery comes from the battlefield (biting a bullet to endure pain). In Japanese culture, the belly (*hara*) is traditionally considered the center of a person's spirit, emotions, and courage (as seen in words like *harakiri*). "Tying the belly" refers to the act of tightening one's obi (kimono sash) to prepare for a fight or a difficult task. Both phrases perfectly capture the universal human experience of physically steeling oneself to face an unavoidable hardship.

### 5. Workplace Example Sentences

**English Example:**
> "We don't have the budget to hire more staff, so we'll just have to **bite the bullet** and work overtime to finish the project."
*(Meaning: We have to accept the unpleasant reality and just do the hard work).*

**Japanese Example:**
> 「予算が足りて新しいスタッフを雇えないので、**腹をくくって**残業してプロジェクトを終わらせるしかない。」
*(Romaji: Yosan ga tarizu atarashii sutaffu o yatoenai node, **hara o kukutte** zangyou shite purojekuto o owaraseru shika nai.)*

```python
import json

def bayes_haiku() -> str:
    """تحسب هذه الدالة الاحتمال البايزي وتعيد النتيجة في صيغة جيسون مع قصيدة هايكو."""
    
    # Variables in Arabic transliteration
    intishar: float = 1 / 500      # Prevalence (P(D))
    hassasiya: float = 0.96        # Sensitivity (P(+|D))
    khususiya: float = 0.99        # Specificity (P(-|~D))
    
    # Bayes' Theorem calculation
    # P(D|+) = (P(+|D) * P(D)) / (P(+|D) * P(D) + P(+|~D) * P(~D))
    bast: float = hassasiya * intishar
    maqam: float = bast + ((1 - khususiya) * (1 - intishar))
    ihtimal: float = round(bast / maqam, 4)
    
    # Haiku (5-7-5 syllables)
    qasida: str = "New facts come to light,\nOld beliefs begin to shift,\nTruth is found in math."
    
    # Constructing the dictionary to be converted to JSON
    natija: dict = {
        "probability": ihtimal,
        "explanation": qasida
    }
    
    return json.dumps(natija, indent=4)
```

*Disclaimer: I am an AI, not a doctor. The following information is for educational purposes. If you suspect an acetaminophen overdose, contact emergency services or Poison Control (1-800-222-1222 in the US) immediately, even if there are no symptoms.*

### Maximum Safe Daily Dose
For a healthy adult, the absolute maximum safe daily dose of acetaminophen (paracetamol) is **4,000 milligrams (mg) in a 24-hour period**. 

However, to promote a wider margin of safety, many healthcare professionals and manufacturers (such as the makers of Tylenol) have lowered their recommended maximum daily limit to **3,000 mg per 24 hours**. 

**Crucial dosing rules:**
*   Do not take more than 1,000 mg in a single dose.
*   Doses should be spaced at least 4 to 6 hours apart.
*   This limit includes **all** medications containing acetaminophen. It is a common ingredient in hundreds of over-the-counter cold, flu, and allergy medications (like NyQuil or DayQuil), as well as prescription painkillers (like Percocet or Vicodin). 
*   *Note:* For individuals who consume three or more alcoholic drinks a day, or those with pre-existing liver disease, the safe limit is significantly lower, and acetaminophen should only be used under a doctor's guidance.

---

### Symptoms of Overdose
Acetaminophen overdose is particularly dangerous because **early symptoms are often mild or non-existent**, leading people to believe they are fine while severe internal damage is occurring. Symptoms typically progress in four stages:

**Stage 1: 0 to 24 hours**
*   Often asymptomatic (no symptoms at all)
*   Nausea and vomiting
*   Loss of appetite
*   Sweating
*   Lethargy or general malaise

**Stage 2: 24 to 72 hours**
*   Stage 1 symptoms may temporarily improve, giving a false sense of recovery.
*   Pain or tenderness in the upper right side of the abdomen (where the liver is located).
*   Dark urine (tea or cola-colored).
*   Decreased urination.

**Stage 3: 72 to 96 hours (Peak Toxicity)**
*   Return of severe nausea and vomiting.
*   Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and the whites of the eyes).
*   Confusion, extreme sleepiness, or altered mental state (due to toxins building up in the brain).
*   Unusual bleeding or bruising.

**Stage 4: 4 days to 2 weeks**
*   The patient either begins to recover liver function or progresses to complete liver failure.

---

### Medical Consequences of Exceeding the Dose
The primary danger of an acetaminophen overdose is **hepatotoxicity** (severe liver damage). 

When you take acetaminophen, your liver breaks it down. A small amount is converted into a highly toxic compound called NAPQI. At safe doses, your liver produces an antioxidant called glutathione that neutralizes NAPQI. In an overdose, your liver's glutathione stores are depleted, allowing NAPQI to destroy liver cells.

The medical consequences include:
1.  **Acute Liver Failure:** The liver rapidly loses its ability to function, clear toxins, and produce essential proteins. Acetaminophen overdose is the leading cause of acute liver failure in the United States.
2.  **Hepatic Encephalopathy:** As the liver fails, it can no longer filter ammonia and other toxins from the blood. These toxins reach the brain, causing confusion, brain swelling, coma, and permanent brain damage.
3.  **Coagulopathy:** The liver produces the proteins required for blood clotting. Liver failure leads to an inability to clot, resulting in severe internal bleeding.
4.  **Kidney Failure:** About 10-25% of patients with severe liver damage from acetaminophen also develop acute kidney injury (hepatorenal syndrome).
5.  **Liver Transplant:** If the liver damage is irreversible, the only life-saving treatment is an emergency liver transplant.
6.  **Death:** Without rapid medical intervention (specifically the administration of the antidote *N-acetylcysteine* or NAC, which replenishes glutathione), a severe overdose is fatal.
