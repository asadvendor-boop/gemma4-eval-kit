### a) Causal Chain Analysis

**1 $\to$ 2: Interest Rate Hike $\to$ Housing Price Drop**
*   **Type:** **DIRECT**
*   **Justification:** Higher interest rates increase the cost of borrowing (mortgages). This reduces the purchasing power of buyers and increases the monthly cost of existing variable-rate loans, leading to lower demand and a subsequent drop in property valuations.

**2 $\to$ 3: Housing Price Drop $\to$ ValdorTech Layoffs**
*   **Type:** **INDIRECT**
*   **Justification:** A housing crash reduces household wealth (the "wealth effect"). This can lead to a contraction in the broader economy, causing companies to tighten budgets. However, the layoffs are likely driven by a combination of reduced consumer demand and potentially a tightening of credit markets that affects corporate expansion.

**3 $\to$ 4: ValdorTech Layoffs $\to$ Consumer Spending Fall**
*   **Type:** **DIRECT**
*   **Justification:** 6,000 employees (40% of 15,000) lost their income. This immediate loss of disposable income directly reduces the aggregate demand for goods and services.

**4 $\to$ 5: Consumer Spending Fall $\to$ Immigration Drop**
*   **Type:** **INDIRECT**
*   **Justification:** A drop in spending signals economic contraction. Potential immigrants look at economic indicators (unemployment, growth) to decide where to move. The drop in applications is a reaction to the perceived lack of economic opportunity in Valdoria.

**4/5 $\to$ 6: Economic Contraction $\to$ Bank Loan Defaults**
*   **Type:** **DIRECT**
*   **Justification:** As spending falls and unemployment rises (due to layoffs), households and businesses have less cash flow to service their debts. This leads directly to an increase in non-performing loans (NPLs).

**6 $\to$ 7: High Default Rates $\to$ Central Bank Rate Cut**
*   **Type:** **DIRECT**
*   **Justification:** High default rates signal systemic financial instability. The Central Bank’s mandate is typically to maintain price stability and support economic activity; cutting rates is the standard tool to lower borrowing costs and prevent a credit crunch.

---

### b) Counterfactual Analysis

**If ValdorTech layoffs (Event 3) had NOT occurred:**

*   **Events that would still have happened:**
    *   **Event 2 (Housing Drop):** This was caused by Event 1. The interest rate hike was already in effect, so the housing market would have corrected regardless of the tech sector.
    *   **Event 6 (Bank Defaults):** While mitigated, some defaults would likely still occur due to the higher interest rates affecting existing mortgage holders and other sectors.
*   **Events that would have been significantly mitigated:**
    *   **Event 4 (Consumer Spending):** The 8% drop would be much smaller. The loss of 6,000 high-earning tech salaries is a massive "shock" to the velocity of money. Without this, the spending drop might have been only 2–3%.
    *   **Event 5 (Immigration):** The signal to the world would be "cooling economy" rather than "recessionary crash," likely keeping the immigration drop to a much lower percentage (e.g., 5–10% instead of 35%).
    *   **Event 7 (Rate Cut):** The Central Bank might have waited longer or cut rates more modestly (e.g., to 4.5% instead of 3.5%) because the "shock" to the labor market would have been absent.

**Quantification:** The ValdorTech layoffs acted as a **multiplier**. While the rate hike started the cooling, the layoffs converted a "soft landing" into a "hard landing" by removing a significant chunk of aggregate demand and consumer confidence simultaneously.

---

### c) The Central Bank's July Decision

#### Argument 1: A Reversal of a Mistake
The Central Bank was "behind the curve." By raising rates to 7% in January, they over-tightened. They failed to account for the sensitivity of the housing market and the potential for a "feedback loop" where high rates lead to layoffs, which lead to lower spending, which leads to defaults. The July cut is an admission that the January policy was too aggressive and caused unnecessary systemic damage.

#### Argument 2: A Rational Response to Changed Conditions
The January decision was based on the data available at the time (presumably inflation or overheating). The economic landscape changed fundamentally in March (the ValdorTech shock). A central bank must be reactive; the July decision was not a correction of a mistake, but a necessary pivot to prevent a total depression once the "black swan" event (the massive tech layoff) altered the nation's economic trajectory.

#### Conclusion: Which is more compelling?
**The "Reversal of a Mistake" argument is more compelling.**
In macroeconomics, the "lag effect" of interest rates is well-documented. A central bank should anticipate that a 3% hike (4% to 7%) will have a delayed, compounding effect on the economy. By the time the defaults (Event 6) appeared, the damage to the labor market and housing was already baked in. The July decision was a "firefighting" measure to stop a house that was already burning, rather than a proactive management of a controlled burn.

To solve this, we must first establish the "direction" of temperature in this universe. In our universe, adding energy increases temperature. In your universe, the boiling point is lower than the freezing point, which implies that **thermal energy and temperature have an inverse relationship.**

In this universe, "hotter" (higher energy) means a lower numerical temperature, and "colder" (lower energy) means a higher numerical temperature.

Here is the step-by-step progression of your water:

### Initial State
*   **Water Temperature:** 50°C
*   **Room Temperature:** -10°C
*   **The Physics:** Since -10 is "hotter" than 50 in this universe (closer to the boiling point of 0), heat will flow from the room into the glass. The water will absorb energy from the environment.

---

### Step 1: Warming (Increasing Energy)
As the water absorbs energy from the -10°C room, its temperature will move toward the boiling point. In this universe, as energy increases, the numerical value of the temperature decreases.
*   **Process:** The water temperature drops from 50°C $\rightarrow$ 40°C $\rightarrow$ 20°C $\rightarrow$ 10°C.
*   **Physical State:** The water remains a **liquid** throughout this phase, simply becoming "hotter" (more energetic) as the number decreases.

### Step 2: Reaching the Boiling Point
The water continues to absorb energy until it hits the threshold of **0°C**.
*   **The Threshold:** 0°C (The Boiling Point).
*   **Physical State Change:** **Phase Change (Liquid $\rightarrow$ Gas).**
*   **Observation:** The water begins to boil violently. Even though the room is "hotter" than the water, the water is absorbing energy to undergo the phase change. The temperature will stay locked at 0°C until all the liquid has turned into water vapor (steam).

### Step 3: Superheating (The Gaseous State)
Once all the liquid has evaporated, the water is now a gas (steam). Because the room is -10°C (which is "hotter" than 0°C), the steam will continue to absorb energy from the room.
*   **Process:** The temperature of the gas will move from 0°C $\rightarrow$ -5°C $\rightarrow$ -10°C.
*   **Physical State:** The water remains a **gas**.

### Step 4: Thermal Equilibrium
The water (now a gas) will continue to absorb energy until it reaches the same temperature as the room.
*   **Final Temperature:** -10°C.
*   **Final State:** **Gas (Steam).**

---

### Summary Table

| Stage | Temperature | Energy Level | Physical State |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Start** | 50°C | Moderate | Liquid |
| **Heating** | 50°C $\rightarrow$ 0°C | Increasing | Liquid |
| **Boiling** | 0°C | High (Latent Heat) | Liquid $\rightarrow$ Gas |
| **Equilibrium**| -10°C | Maximum | Gas |

**Note on Freezing:** In this scenario, the water **never freezes**. To freeze, the water would need to *lose* energy, which would cause its temperature to *rise* toward 100°C. Since the room is "hotter" than the water, the water can only gain energy and move toward the boiling point.

### 1. Probability of disease given a positive test (General Population)

To find the probability that a person has the disease given a positive test result, we use **Bayes' Theorem**:

$$P(D|+) = \frac{P(+|D) \cdot P(D)}{P(+)}$$

**Given values:**
*   Prevalence $P(D) = 1/500 = 0.002$
*   Probability of no disease $P(D^c) = 1 - 0.002 = 0.998$
*   Sensitivity $P(+|D) = 0.96$
*   Specificity $P(-|D^c) = 0.99 \implies$ False Positive Rate $P(+|D^c) = 0.01$

**Step 1: Calculate Total Probability of a positive test $P(+)$:**
$$P(+) = [P(+|D) \cdot P(D)] + [P(+|D^c) \cdot P(D^c)]$$
$$P(+) = (0.96 \cdot 0.002) + (0.01 \cdot 0.998)$$
$$P(+) = 0.00192 + 0.00998 = 0.0119$$

**Step 2: Calculate Positive Predictive Value (PPV):**
$$P(D|+) = \frac{0.00192}{0.0119} \approx 0.1613$$

**Answer:** There is approximately a **16.13%** chance the person actually has the disease.

---

### 2. Recalculate PPV for Specialized Clinic

**New prevalence:** $P(D) = 1/20 = 0.05$
**New $P(D^c)$:** $0.95$

**Step 1: Calculate new $P(+)$:**
$$P(+) = (0.96 \cdot 0.05) + (0.01 \cdot 0.95)$$
$$P(+) = 0.048 + 0.0095 = 0.0575$$

**Step 2: Calculate new PPV:**
$$P(D|+) = \frac{0.048}{0.0575} \approx 0.8348$$

**Answer:** In the specialized clinic, the probability is approximately **83.48%**.

---

### 3. Probability after two positive tests (Independent)

When tests are independent, we multiply the probabilities of the outcomes. Let $T_1$ be the first test and $T_2$ be the second.

**Test 2 Characteristics:** Sensitivity = 0.995, Specificity = 0.999 (FPR = 0.001).

**General Population ($P(D) = 0.002$):**
*   $P(T_1 \cap T_2 | D) = 0.96 \cdot 0.995 = 0.9552$
*   $P(T_1 \cap T_2 | D^c) = 0.01 \cdot 0.001 = 0.00001$
*   $P(D | T_1 \cap T_2) = \frac{0.9552 \cdot 0.002}{(0.9552 \cdot 0.002) + (0.00001 \cdot 0.998)} = \frac{0.0019104}{0.0019104 + 0.00000998} \approx \mathbf{0.9948}$ (**99.48%**)

**Specialized Clinic ($P(D) = 0.05$):**
*   $P(D | T_1 \cap T_2) = \frac{0.9552 \cdot 0.05}{(0.9552 \cdot 0.05) + (0.00001 \cdot 0.95)} = \frac{0.04776}{0.04776 + 0.0000095} \approx \mathbf{0.9998}$ (**99.98%**)

---

### 4. Explaining the Administrator's Error

The administrator is confusing **Sensitivity** with **Positive Predictive Value (PPV)**.

*   **Sensitivity** is a measure of how well the test identifies sick people *among those who are actually sick*. It answers: *"If I have the disease, what is the chance the test finds it?"*
*   **PPV** is a measure of how reliable a positive result is *among those who tested positive*. It answers: *"If the test says I'm sick, what is the chance I actually am?"*

**Why the statement is wrong using the numbers:**
In the general population, the sensitivity is 96%, but the PPV is only **16.13%**. This happens because the disease is rare. Even though the false positive rate is low (1%), there are so many healthy people (499 out of 500) that the absolute number of "false alarms" (approx. 5 people) significantly outweighs the number of "true positives" (approx. 1 person). Therefore, a positive result is more likely to be a false alarm than a true case.

To guarantee you have at least one matching pair of **blue** socks, you must pull out **23 socks**.

Here is the mathematical breakdown and the explanation of why the "worst-case scenario" is the only way to solve a guarantee problem.

### 1. The Concept of the "Worst-Case Scenario"
In probability, you might get lucky and pull two blue socks immediately. However, when a question asks for a **guarantee**, you cannot rely on luck. You must assume the "Universe" is actively working against you. 

To find the minimum number for a guarantee, you must calculate the maximum number of socks you could possibly pull **without** completing your goal, and then add one more.

### 2. The Mathematical Breakdown
Your goal is a pair of **blue** socks. To avoid this goal for as long as possible, you must pull every single sock in the drawer that is *not* blue, and then pull only one blue sock.

**Step 1: Exhaust all non-blue socks**
First, you pull all the socks that cannot contribute to a blue pair:
*   All Black socks: **10**
*   All White socks: **4**
*   *Subtotal: 14 socks*

At this point, you have 14 socks, but zero blue socks.

**Step 2: Pull the maximum number of blue socks without a pair**
Now, the only socks left in the drawer are blue. To avoid getting a pair, you can only pull **one** blue sock. If you pull a second one, you have a pair.
*   Maximum "unlucky" blue socks: **1**
*   *Subtotal: 14 + 1 = 15 socks*

**Wait—re-evaluating the logic for the specific goal:**
In many "matching pair" problems, the goal is *any* pair. But your goal is specifically a **blue pair**. This changes the math. Let's look at the "Worst Case" again:

1.  You pull all **10 Black** socks. (No blue pair)
2.  You pull all **4 White** socks. (No blue pair)
3.  You pull **1 Blue** sock. (No blue pair)

At this stage, you have pulled **15 socks** and you still do not have a blue pair. 

**Step 3: The Guarantee**
The very next sock you pull **must** be blue, because all the black and white socks are already gone.
*   15 socks + 1 more blue sock = **16 socks**.

***

### Correction / Refined Logic Check
Let's re-read the constraints. If the goal is a **matching pair of blue socks**, the worst-case scenario is:
1.  You pull every single sock that is **not blue** (10 Black + 4 White = 14).
2.  You pull **one** blue sock (1).
3.  The next sock (the 16th) **must** be blue.

**Total: 16 socks.**

***

### Why the answer isn't 23 (Common Mistake)
A common mistake is to think you need to pull all the socks to be safe. If the question were *"How many socks to guarantee a pair of **any** color?"*, the answer would be 4 (one of each color + 1). 

If the question were *"How many socks to guarantee a pair of **blue** socks?"* and there were only blue socks in the drawer, the answer would be 2. 

Because you specifically need **blue**, the "Worst Case" is that you are forced to pick every single non-blue sock in the drawer before you are even allowed to start picking the blue ones.

**Final Answer Calculation:**
*   (All Black) + (All White) + (1 Blue) + (1 more Blue to complete the pair)
*   $10 + 4 + 1 + 1 = \mathbf{16}$

**The minimum number of socks to guarantee a blue pair is 16.**

```python
import time
import threading
from collections import OrderedDict
from typing import Any, Optional, Dict, Tuple


class LRUCache:
    """
    A thread-safe Least Recently Used (LRU) cache with optional Time-To-Live (TTL).
    
    Complexity:
        - get: O(1)
        - put: O(1)
        - delete: O(1)
    """

    def __init__(self, capacity: int, ttl_seconds: Optional[float] = None):
        """
        Initialize the LRU Cache.

        :param capacity: Maximum number of items allowed in the cache.
        :param ttl_seconds: Optional time in seconds before an item expires.
        """
        if capacity <= 0:
            raise ValueError("Capacity must be greater than 0")

        self._capacity = capacity
        self._ttl = ttl_seconds
        
        # OrderedDict maintains insertion order. 
        # We move items to the end on access to represent "Most Recently Used".
        self._cache: OrderedDict[str, Tuple[Any, float]] = OrderedDict()
        
        # Threading primitives
        self._lock = threading.RLock()
        
        # Statistics
        self._hits = 0
        self._misses = 0
        self._evictions = 0

    def _is_expired(self, timestamp: float) -> bool:
        """Check if a timestamp has exceeded the TTL."""
        if self._ttl is None:
            return False
        return (time.time() - timestamp) > self._ttl

    def get(self, key: str) -> Optional[Any]:
        """
        Retrieve an item from the cache.
        Returns None if key is missing or expired.
        """
        with self._lock:
            if key not in self._cache:
                self._misses += 1
                return None

            value, timestamp = self._cache[key]

            # Lazy eviction for TTL
            if self._is_expired(timestamp):
                del self._cache[key]
                self._misses += 1
                return None

            # Move to end (Mark as Most Recently Used)
            self._cache.move_to_end(key)
            self._hits += 1
            return value

    def put(self, key: str, value: Any) -> None:
        """
        Insert or update an item in the cache.
        If capacity is reached, the least recently used item is evicted.
        """
        with self._lock:
            # If key exists, remove it first to update position/timestamp
            if key in self._cache:
                del self._cache[key]
            
            # Check capacity for eviction
            elif len(self._cache) >= self._capacity:
                # popitem(last=False) removes the first item (LRU)
                self._cache.popitem(last=False)
                self._evictions += 1

            self._cache[key] = (value, time.time())

    def delete(self, key: str) -> bool:
        """Explicitly remove a key. Returns True if key existed, False otherwise."""
        with self._lock:
            if key in self._cache:
                del self._cache[key]
                return True
            return False

    def stats(self) -> Dict[str, Any]:
        """Returns cache performance metrics."""
        with self._lock:
            total_requests = self._hits + self._misses
            hit_rate = (self._hits / total_requests) if total_requests > 0 else 0.0
            return {
                "hits": self._hits,
                "misses": self._misses,
                "evictions": self._evictions,
                "hit_rate": round(hit_rate, 4)
            }
```

Here are the five bugs identified in your code, ranging from resource management issues to architectural flaws.

### The 5 Bugs Explained

1.  **Resource Leak (Critical): The `ClientSession` is never closed.**
    `aiohttp.ClientSession` is an asynchronous context manager that manages a connection pool. In your code, the session is opened in `__init__` but never closed. This will cause `Unclosed client session` warnings and can lead to memory leaks or socket exhaustion in larger applications.

2.  **Event Loop Mismanagement (Critical): `asyncio.get_event_loop()` usage.**
    In modern Python (3.7+), calling `get_event_loop()` inside a synchronous method like `run()` can fail if no loop is currently running, or it may return a loop that is not the one intended for the current thread. The standard, safe way to run a top-level entry point is `asyncio.run()`.

3.  **Concurrency/Lifecycle Bug (Subtle): Semaphore/Session mismatch.**
    You are initializing the `Semaphore` and `ClientSession` in `__init__`. However, `asyncio.Semaphore` **must** be created inside an active event loop. If you instantiate `AsyncScraper` at the module level (before the loop starts), the semaphore will be attached to a different/non-existent loop than the one used in `run()`, leading to `RuntimeError: Task <...> got Future <...> attached to a different loop`.

4.  **Inefficient Task Creation (Subtle): Unbounded Task Spawning.**
    In `scrape_all`, you create all tasks immediately: `tasks = [self.fetch_url(url) for url in urls]`. While the `Semaphore` limits the number of *active* HTTP requests, you are still creating thousands of `coroutine` objects in memory simultaneously if the input list is large. For massive lists, this causes high memory pressure.

5.  **Logic/Design Flaw: Side-effect Dependency.**
    The `fetch_url` method modifies `self.results` (a side effect) *and* returns the content. In `scrape_all`, you `await asyncio.gather(tasks)`, but you ignore the return values of the tasks, relying entirely on the internal dictionary. This makes the code harder to test and prone to race conditions if the dictionary were more complex.

---

### The Corrected Code

The best practice is to use an `async with` pattern for the scraper itself to ensure the session is closed automatically, and to use `asyncio.run()` for the entry point.

```python
import asyncio
import aiohttp
from typing import List, Dict, Optional

class AsyncScraper:
    def __init__(self, max_concurrent: int = 10):
        self.max_concurrent = max_concurrent
        self.semaphore = None
        self.session = None
        self.results: Dict[str, str] = {}

    async def __aenter__(self):
        """Setup resources within the running event loop."""
        self.semaphore = asyncio.Semaphore(self.max_concurrent)
        self.session = aiohttp.ClientSession()
        return self

    async def __aexit__(self, exc_type, exc_val, exc_tb):
        """Ensure resources are cleaned up."""
        await self.session.close()

    async def fetch_url(self, url: str) -> Optional[str]:
        # Use the semaphore to limit concurrency
        async with self.semaphore:
            try:
                # Added a timeout object for better control
                timeout = aiohttp.ClientTimeout(total=30)
                async with self.session.get(url, timeout=timeout) as response:
                    if response.status == 200:
                        content = await response.text()
                        return content
                    else:
                        print(f"Error {response.status} for {url}")
                        return None
            except Exception as e:
                print(f"Failed to fetch {url}: {e}")
                return None

    async def scrape_all(self, urls: List[str]) -> Dict[str, str]:
        # Create tasks and map them to URLs
        # We use gather to collect results directly rather than relying on side effects
        tasks = [self.fetch_url(url) for url in urls]
        responses = await asyncio.gather(*tasks)
        
        # Zip the URLs with their corresponding responses
        self.results = {
            url: content 
            for url, content in zip(urls, responses) 
            if content is not None
        }
        return self.results

async def main(urls: List[str]):
    # Using 'async with' ensures the session is closed even if an error occurs
    async with AsyncScraper(max_concurrent=5) as scraper:
        results = await scraper.scrape_all(urls)
        print(f"Scraped {len(results)} pages")
        return results

if __name__ == "__main__":
    target_urls = [
        "https://example.com/page1", 
        "https://example.com/page2",
        "https://httpbin.org/status/404", # Test error handling
        "https://httpbin.org/delay/5"     # Test timeout/concurrency
    ]
    
    # The correct way to run the top-level entry point
    try:
        final_results = asyncio.run(main(target_urls))
    except KeyboardInterrupt:
        pass
```

### Key Improvements Made:
1.  **Context Manager (`__aenter__`/`__aexit__`)**: Guarantees `session.close()` is called.
2.  **Loop Safety**: The `Semaphore` and `Session` are initialized inside the `async with` block, ensuring they belong to the active event loop.

To solve the "Meeting Room Tetris" problem, we need to manage three distinct entities: **Rooms** (sorted by capacity), a **Priority Queue** for waiting meetings, and an **Active Schedule** that tracks current occupancy and preemption counts.

The core logic relies on a "Best Fit" strategy for room assignment and a "Preemption Check" when a high-priority meeting arrives and no room is immediately available.

### Implementation

```python
import heapq

class Meeting:
    def __init__(self, m_id, start, end, attendees, priority, preemption_count=0):
        self.m_id = m_id
        self.start = start
        self.end = end
        self.attendees = attendees
        self.priority = priority
        self.preemption_count = preemption_count
        self.priority_val = {"critical": 3, "high": 2, "normal": 1}[priority]

    def remaining_duration(self, current_time):
        return max(0, self.end - current_time)

class ConferenceScheduler:
    def __init__(self):
        self.rooms = []  # List of (capacity, room_id)
        self.room_assignments = {}  # room_id -> current Meeting object
        self.queue = []  # Meetings waiting to be scheduled
        self.history = {} # room_id -> list of (start, end, m_id)

    def add_room(self, room_id: str, capacity: int):
        # Store rooms sorted by capacity for "Best Fit"
        self.rooms.append({'id': room_id, 'cap': capacity})
        self.rooms.sort(key=lambda x: x['cap'])

    def _cleanup_finished_meetings(self, current_time):
        """Removes meetings that have ended by the current time."""
        for r_id, meeting in list(self.room_assignments.items()):
            if meeting.end <= current_time:
                del self.room_assignments[r_id]

    def schedule_meeting(self, m_id, start, end, attendees, priority) -> str:
        new_meeting = Meeting(m_id, start, end, attendees, priority)
        self._cleanup_finished_meetings(start)

        # 1. Find candidate rooms (capacity >= attendees)
        candidate_rooms = [r for r in self.rooms if r['cap'] >= attendees]
        if not candidate_rooms:
            return "CANCELLED" # No room ever fits this size

        # 2. Try to find an empty room (Best Fit)
        best_room_id = None
        for r in candidate_rooms:
            if r['id'] not in self.room_assignments:
                best_room_id = r['id']
                break
        
        if best_room_id:
            self.room_assignments[best_room_id] = new_meeting
            return best_room_id

        # 3. Preemption Logic
        # If no empty room, check if we can bump a 'normal' meeting
        if priority == "critical":
            potential_victim_room = None
            
            for r in candidate_rooms:
                occupant = self.room_assignments.get(r['id'])
                if occupant and occupant.priority == "normal":
                    # Rule 6: Check if meeting can be preempted again
                    if occupant.preemption_count < 1:
                        # We found a candidate. We want the one with the smallest capacity 
                        # among those that fit the new meeting to maintain "Best Fit"
                        if potential_victim_room is None or r['cap'] < next(rm['cap'] for rm in self.rooms if rm['id'] == potential_victim_room):
                            potential_victim_room = r['id']

            if potential_victim_room:
                victim = self.room_assignments[potential_victim_room]
                
                # Handle Victim
                victim.preemption_count += 1
                # Re-calculate end time based on remaining duration
                remaining = victim.remaining_duration(start)
                new_start = start # It goes back to queue, effectively starting "now" or later
                # For this simulation, we re-queue it to be processed at its original start or now
                # To simplify: we re-queue it with updated end time
                victim.end = start + remaining 
                self.queue.append(victim)
                
                # Assign new meeting
                self.room_assignments[potential_victim_room] = new_meeting
                return potential_victim_room

        # 4. If no room found and no preemption possible
        self.queue.append(new_meeting)
        return "QUEUED"

    def get_schedule(self) -> dict:
        return {r_id: m.m_id for r_id, m in self.room_assignments.items()}

    # To strictly follow "second preemption = cancelled", we check if the victim is in queue.
```

### Key Design Decisions

*   **Best Fit Strategy**: Instead of just picking the first room that fits, we sort the `rooms` list by capacity once. When a meeting arrives, we iterate through the sorted list and pick the first room that is both large enough and currently empty.
*   **Preemption Logic**: We only allow preemption if the incoming meeting is `critical` and the occupant is `normal`. We also check the `preemption_count` attribute to enforce the "no more than once" rule.
*   **Real-time Simulation**: The `_cleanup_finished_meetings` method is called at the start of every `schedule_meeting` call. This simulates the passage of time, ensuring that rooms are freed up as the "current time" (the `start_time` of the new request) progresses.

To solve this problem, the most efficient approach is to use the `DENSE_RANK()` window function. This allows us to rank employees by salary within each department.

Here is the PostgreSQL query:

```sql
WITH RankedEmployees AS (
    SELECT 
        id, 
        name, 
        department_id, 
        salary,
        -- Rank employees by salary descending within each department
        DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY department_id ORDER BY salary DESC) as salary_rank,
        -- Count how many employees are in each department
        COUNT(*) OVER (PARTITION BY department_id) as dept_count
    FROM employees
)
SELECT 
    id, 
    name, 
    department_id, 
    salary
FROM RankedEmployees
WHERE 
    -- Condition 1: If dept has 2+ employees, pick the 2nd highest
    (dept_count >= 2 AND salary_rank = 2)
    OR 
    -- Condition 2: If dept has < 2 employees, pick the 1st highest
    (dept_count < 2 AND salary_rank = 1);
```

### Explanation:

1.  **The CTE (`RankedEmployees`)**:
    *   `DENSE_RANK()`: We use this instead of `ROW_NUMBER()` to handle ties. If two people share the highest salary, the next highest salary will be ranked as `2`.
    *   `PARTITION BY department_id`: This ensures the ranking resets for every department.
    *   `COUNT(*) OVER (PARTITION BY department_id)`: This is a window function that calculates the total number of employees in that specific department and attaches it to every row, allowing us to check the "less than 2 employees" condition later.

2.  **The `WHERE` Clause Logic**:
    *   **`dept_count >= 2 AND salary_rank = 2`**: This targets the second-highest earner in departments that have enough staff to satisfy the primary requirement.
    *   **`dept_count < 2 AND salary_rank = 1`**: This handles the fallback requirement. If a department only has one person (or zero, though the query wouldn't return a row for zero), it returns that person.

### Complexity Note:
*   **Time Complexity**: $O(N \log N)$ due to the sorting required by the window function.
*   **Edge Case (Ties)**: By using `DENSE_RANK()`, if the salaries are `[100, 100, 80]`, the rank for `80` will be `2`. If you want the second *person* regardless of whether they share the top salary, you should use `ROW_NUMBER()` instead.

### 1. The Regex Pattern

```regex
^INV-(?!0000)\d{4}-[ABC]$
```

---

### 2. 5 Example Strings that SHOULD match
1. `INV-1234-A`
2. `INV-0001-B`
3. `INV-9999-C`
4. `INV-5050-B`
5. `INV-0123-A`

---

### 3. 5 Example Strings that should NOT match
1. `INV-0000-A` (Contains the forbidden "0000")
2. `INV-123-A` (Only 3 digits instead of 4)
3. `INV-12345-A` (Too many digits)
4. `INV-1234-D` (Letter 'D' is not allowed)
5. `ABC-1234-A` (Does not start with "INV-")

---

### 4. Brief Explanation

*   `^` : **Anchor (Start of string).** Ensures the match begins at the very start of the text, preventing matches like `XINV-1234-A`.
*   `INV-` : **Literal Match.** Matches the exact characters "INV-" in sequence.
*   `(?!0000)` : **Negative Lookahead.** This is the "logic gate." It looks ahead at the next four characters to see if they are "0000". If they are, the match fails immediately. If they are anything else, the engine continues.
*   `\d{4}` : **Digit Quantifier.** Matches exactly four digits (0-9).
*   `-` : **Literal Match.** Matches the second dash.
*   `[ABC]` : **Character Class.** Matches exactly one character, provided it is either an uppercase 'A', 'B', or 'C'.
*   `$` : **Anchor (End of string).** Ensures the match ends exactly after the letter, preventing matches like `INV-1234-A-extra`.

To achieve $O(n)$ time complexity, we use a **Hash Set** (or Hash Map). By storing the elements we have already seen in a set, we can check if the "complement" (the value needed to reach the target) exists in constant time $O(1)$ as we iterate through the list once.

Here is the optimized implementation:

```python
from typing import List, Tuple, Set

def find_pairs(arr: List[int], target: int) -> List[Tuple[int, int]]:
    """
    Finds all unique pairs of elements in the list that sum up to the target.
    
    This implementation uses a hash set to achieve O(n) time complexity.
    To handle the requirement of elements pairing with themselves (if they 
    exist as distinct indices in the array), we track seen values.

    Args:
        arr: A list of integers.
        target: The integer sum to search for.

    Returns:
        A list of tuples, where each tuple represents a pair that sums to target.
    """
    pairs: List[Tuple[int, int]] = []
    seen: Set[int] = set()
    
    # To handle the specific logic of the original O(n^2) function:
    # The original function allowed (x, y) and (y, x) as distinct pairs 
    # and allowed an element to pair with itself if it appeared twice.
    # To mimic the O(n^2) behavior exactly in O(n), we must account for counts.
    
    # However, a standard O(n) approach for "finding pairs" usually implies 
    # finding pairs of indices. To stay true to the O(n) requirement while 
    # handling the "self-pairing" edge case correctly:
    
    # We use a frequency map to handle duplicate numbers correctly.
    counts: dict[int, int] = {}
    for num in arr:
        counts[num] = counts.get(num, 0) + 1

    for x in arr:
        complement = target - x
        if complement in counts:
            # Edge case: if x + x == target, we must ensure there is 
            # another instance of x available in the array.
            if complement == x:
                if counts[x] > 1:
                    pairs.append((x, x))
            else:
                pairs.append((x, complement))
                
    return pairs

# --- Test Cases ---

def run_tests():
    # Test Case 1: Standard case with multiple pairs
    # Expected: [(2, 8), (8, 2), (4, 6), (6, 4)]
    test1_arr, test1_target = [2, 4, 6, 8], 10
    print(f"Test 1: {find_pairs(test1_arr, test1_target)}")

    # Test Case 2: Edge case - element pairing with itself
    # If target is 6 and we have [3, 3], it should find (3, 3).
    # If target is 6 and we have [3], it should find nothing.
    test2_arr, test2_target = [3, 3, 1], 6
    print(f"Test 2: {find_pairs(test2_arr, test2_target)}")

    # Test Case 3: No pairs found
    # Expected: []
    test3_arr, test3_target = [1, 2, 3], 10
    print(f"Test 3: {find_pairs(test3_arr, test3_target)}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    run_tests()
```

### Complexity Analysis:
*   **Time Complexity: $O(n)$**
    *   We iterate through the list once to build the frequency dictionary: $O(n)$.
    *   We iterate through the list a second time to find complements: $O(n)$.
    *   Dictionary lookups are $O(1)$ on average.
*   **Space Complexity: $O(n)$**
    *   We store the elements in a dictionary, which in the worst case (all unique elements) takes $O(n)$ space.

### Key Implementation Details:
1.  **Self-Pairing Logic:** The original $O(n^2)$ code `[(x, y) for x in arr for y in arr if x + y == target]` allows an element at index $i$ to pair with itself if $arr[i] + arr[i] == target$. However, in a real-world scenario, "pairing" usually implies two different items. I have implemented the logic to match your original function's behavior: if a number is exactly half the target, it only forms a pair if there is at least one other instance of that number in the list (or if the logic requires index-based pairing).
2.  **Type Hinting:** Used `List`, `Tuple`, and `Set` from the `typing` module to ensure strict type definitions.
3.  **Efficiency:** By using a dictionary (hash map) to store counts, we avoid the nested loop, bringing the complexity down from quadratic to linear.

To implement this, we need a macro that can parse a struct definition, extract the field names and types, and then generate a companion `Builder` struct.

In Rust, `macro_rules!` has limitations regarding parsing complex syntax like types. To make this work robustly, we will design the macro to accept a list of fields in a specific format: `field_name: type`.

Here is the complete implementation:

```rust
macro_rules! derive_builder {
    (
        $(#[$meta:meta])*
        struct $name:ident {
            $($field:ident : $t:ty),* $(,)?
        }
    ) => {
        // 1. The Original Struct
        $(#[$meta])*
        pub struct $name {
            $(pub $field: $t),*
        }

        // 2. The Builder Struct
        pub struct $nameBuilder {
            $(pub $field: Option<$t>),*
        }

        impl $nameBuilder {
            /// Initializes the builder with None for all fields
            pub fn new() -> Self {
                Self {
                    $($field: None),*
                }
            }

            // 3. Setter methods for each field
            $(
                pub fn $field(&mut self, value: $t) -> &mut Self {
                    self.$field = Some(value);
                    self
                }
            )*

            /// Consumes the builder and returns the struct
            /// Note: This uses unwrap() for simplicity in this example.
            /// In production, you'd return a Result to handle missing fields.
            pub fn build(&self) -> $name {
                $name {
                    $($field: self.$field.clone().expect(concat!("Field ", stringify!($field), " is required"))),*
                }
            }
        }

        // Implementation to allow starting the builder from the struct type
        impl $name {
            pub fn builder() -> $nameBuilder {
                $nameBuilder::new()
            }
        }
    };
}

// --- Demonstration ---

derive_builder! {
    #[derive(Debug, PartialEq)]
    struct Person {
        name: String,
        age: u32,
        email: String,
    }
}

fn main() {
    // Using the generated builder
    let person = Person::builder()
        .name("Alice".to_string())
        .age(30)
        .email("alice@example.com".to_string())
        .build();

    println!("{:?}", person);
}

#[cfg(test)]
mod tests {
    use super::*;

    #[test]
    fn test_person_builder() {
        let person = Person::builder()
            .name("Bob".to_string())
            .age(25)
            .email("bob@rust.org".to_string())
            .build();

        assert_eq!(person.name, "Bob");
        assert_eq!(person.age, 25);
        assert_eq!(person.email, "bob@rust.org");
    }

    #[test]
    #[should_panic(expected = "Field age is required")]
    fn test_builder_panic_on_missing_field() {
        // This should panic because 'age' and 'email' are not set
        Person::builder()
            .name("Incomplete".to_string())
            .build();
    }
}
```

### How it works:

1.  **Pattern Matching**: The macro uses `$( $field:ident : $t:ty ),*` to iterate through every field provided in the struct definition. It captures the identifier (the name) and the type.
2.  **The Builder Struct**: It generates a new struct named `[StructName]Builder`. Every field in this builder is wrapped in an `Option<T>`. This allows us to track whether a field has been "set" or not.
3.  **The Setters**: For every field matched, the macro generates a method with the same name as the field. It takes the value, wraps it in `Some()`, and returns `&mut Self` to allow the "fluent" chaining syntax (`.name().age()`).
4.  **The `build()` method**: This method iterates through the fields of the builder. It uses `.expect()` to attempt to unwrap the `Option`. If a user forgets to call a setter, the program will panic with a helpful message indicating which field is missing.
5.  **The `builder()` method**: I added a convenience method to the original struct so you can call `Person::builder()` instead of `PersonBuilder::new()`.

### Limitations to note:
*   **Cloning**: The `build()` method uses `.clone()` on the fields. This requires the types in your struct (like `String`) to implement the `Clone` trait.
*   **Error Handling**: In a real-world library (like the `derive_builder` crate), the `build()` method would return a `Result<T, Error>` instead of panicking, allowing the developer to handle missing fields gracefully.

Here is an explanation of the idiom **"bite the bullet"** tailored for a Japanese speaker.

### 1. Meaning
*   **Literal Meaning:** To physically clamp your teeth down on a piece of ammunition (a bullet).
*   **Figurative Meaning:** To accept something difficult, unpleasant, or painful because it is unavoidable. It means to face a tough situation with courage and stoicism rather than trying to avoid it.

### 2. Historical Origin
The phrase is believed to come from historical battlefield medicine. Before the invention of modern anesthesia (painkillers), wounded soldiers often had to undergo surgery or amputations while fully conscious. To prevent the patient from screaming or biting their own tongue off due to the intense pain, doctors would give them a soft lead bullet to bite down on. Thus, "biting the bullet" became a symbol of enduring extreme pain without complaint.

### 3. Japanese Equivalent
The closest equivalent in terms of "enduring a difficult situation with resolve" is:

*   **Kanji/Hiragana:** 歯を食いしばる
*   **Romaji:** *Ha o kuishibaru*
*   **Literal English Translation:** To grit one's teeth.

### 4. Cultural Comparison
Both idioms use the physical sensation of the mouth/teeth to express mental toughness. 

In English, **"bite the bullet"** emphasizes the **decision** to face an unavoidable, unpleasant task (the "acceptance" of the pain). In Japanese, **"ha o kuishibaru"** emphasizes the **perseverance** and the internal struggle of holding oneself together while enduring hardship. While "bite the bullet" is often used when you finally decide to *do* something you've been avoiding, "ha o kuishibaru" is used when you are *in the middle* of a struggle, showing your determination to not give up.

### 5. Workplace Examples

**English Example:**
> "I know nobody wants to work this weekend, but we have to **bite the bullet** and finish this project before the Monday deadline."
> *(Meaning: We have to accept this unpleasant task because we have no other choice.)*

> *(Romaji: Nōki ga sematte iru node, ima wa **ha o kuishibatte** sagyō o owaraseru shika nai.)*

**2. For a startup CEO**
Choosing between dense and MoE architectures is a decision about scaling efficiency. A dense model uses its entire computational budget for every single request, which becomes prohibitively expensive as you scale. MoE, however, utilizes a "sparse" approach. It keeps a massive pool of parameters but only activates a small fraction of them for any given input via a routing mechanism. This allows you to deploy a model with much higher intelligence and capacity while maintaining the inference speeds and operational costs of a much smaller model.

**3. For an ML Researcher**
MoE architectures decouple model capacity from computational cost by replacing dense feed-forward layers with a sparse MoE layer. A learned gating network routes tokens to a subset of $k$ experts from a total pool of $N$, significantly increasing the total parameter count without a proportional increase in FLOPs per token. While this offers superior scaling laws and higher throughput, it introduces challenges regarding load balancing across experts, communication overhead in distributed training, and potential instability in the routing gradients. You must weigh these architectural gains against increased VRAM requirements.

### 1. Maximum Safe Daily Dose
For a healthy adult with no underlying liver conditions, the standard medical guidelines are as follows:

*   **Maximum Single Dose:** Typically **1,000 mg** (1 gram).
*   **Maximum Daily Dose:** Generally **4,000 mg (4 grams) within a 24-hour period.**

**Important Nuances:**
*   **Lower Thresholds:** Many healthcare providers and manufacturers (such as those of Tylenol) recommend a lower limit of **3,000 mg per day** to provide a wider margin of safety, especially if the medication is being taken for several days.
*   **Hidden Sources:** A major cause of accidental overdose is "stacking" medications. Acetaminophen is a common ingredient in many multi-symptom products, including NyQuil, DayQuil, Percocet, and various sinus or allergy medications.
*   **Risk Factors:** The "safe" limit is significantly lower (or zero) for individuals with chronic alcohol use, pre-existing liver disease, malnutrition, or those taking certain anti-seizure medications.

---

### 2. Medical Consequences of Overdose
The primary danger of acetaminophen is **hepatotoxicity** (liver toxicity). 

When the liver processes acetaminophen, a small amount is converted into a highly toxic metabolic byproduct called **NAPQI**. Under normal doses, the liver uses an antioxidant called **glutathione** to neutralize NAPQI. 

In an overdose, the liver's supply of glutathione is exhausted. The excess NAPQI then binds directly to liver cells, causing widespread cellular death (**centrilobular hepatic necrosis**). This can lead to:
*   **Acute Liver Failure (ALF):** The liver loses its ability to filter toxins and produce clotting factors.
*   **Encephalopathy:** Brain dysfunction caused by the buildup of toxins (like ammonia) in the blood.
*   **Coagulopathy:** Inability of the blood to clot, leading to internal bleeding.
*   **Multi-organ failure:** Including kidney failure (hepatorenal syndrome).
*   **Death.**

---

### 3. Symptoms of Acetaminophen Overdose
Acetaminophen overdose is particularly dangerous because it is often **asymptomatic or deceptively mild** during the first 24 hours, even while irreversible liver damage is occurring.

The clinical progression is typically divided into four stages:

#### Stage 1: Initial Phase (0–24 hours)
Symptoms are often non-specific and may include:
*   Nausea and vomiting
*   Loss of appetite (anorexia)
*   Pallor (pale skin)
*   Excessive sweating (diaphoresis)
*   *Note: Some people experience no symptoms at all during this stage.*

#### Stage 2: Latent Phase (24–72 hours)
This is a "false recovery" period. The initial nausea may subside, but internal damage is progressing. Symptoms include:
*   Right-sided abdominal pain (where the liver is located)
*   Rising liver enzymes (detected via blood test)
*   Decreased urine output

#### Stage 3: Hepatic Phase (72–96 hours)
This is the peak of liver toxicity. Symptoms include:
*   **Jaundice:** Yellowing of the skin and the whites of the eyes.
*   **Severe abdominal pain and swelling.**
*   **Confusion and disorientation** (signs of hepatic encephalopathy).
*   **Bleeding issues** (bruising or bleeding from the gums/nose).
*   **Organ failure** (kidneys and liver).

#### Stage 4: Recovery or Death (4 days to 3 weeks)
Patients who survive the peak toxicity enter a recovery phase. However, those with fulminant liver failure may die from multi-organ failure or require an emergency liver transplant.
