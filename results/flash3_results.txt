In this inverted thermodynamic universe, heat still flows from a warmer object to a colder object, but the phase transitions of water are reversed. Since the room is at -10°C and the water is at 50°C, the water will lose thermal energy to the environment.

Here is the step-by-step physical progression of the water:

### 1. The Liquid Phase (50°C down to 0°C)
Initially, the water is a liquid. In this universe, 50°C is exactly the midpoint between the freezing point (100°C) and the boiling point (0°C). As the water sits in the -10°C room, it begins to lose heat. 
*   **Physical Change:** The temperature of the liquid drops.
*   **Observation:** To an observer from our universe, the water is getting "colder," but it is actually moving toward its **boiling point.**

### 2. The Boiling Point (Reached at 0°C)
Once the water cools down to 0°C, it reaches its phase change threshold. In this universe, 0°C is the boiling point.
*   **Physical Change:** The water begins to undergo a phase transition from **liquid to gas (steam).**
*   **The Plateau:** The temperature of the water will stay at exactly 0°C until every molecule has gained enough latent heat of vaporization to turn into gas. 
*   **Observation:** Even though the room is "freezing" by our standards (-10°C), the water begins to bubble and turn into steam because it has reached its boiling threshold.

### 3. The Gaseous Phase (0°C down to -10°C)
Once all the liquid has evaporated into steam, the steam continues to lose heat to the -10°C room.
*   **Physical Change:** The temperature of the steam drops from 0°C toward -10°C.
*   **Observation:** You now have "cold steam" filling the room or the top of the glass.

### 4. Thermal Equilibrium (at -10°C)
The process stops when the steam reaches -10°C, matching the room's temperature.
*   **Final State:** You are left with **super-cooled gas.**

***

### Summary of the Paradox
If you had moved the water in the opposite direction (into a room at 110°C), the water would have **frozen solid.** 

In your specific scenario (moving 50°C water into a -10°C room), the water **boiled away into vapor** because it moved toward the lower temperature threshold. In this universe, "cooling down" leads to evaporation, and "heating up" leads to solidification.

To solve these problems, we use **Bayes' Theorem**, which relates conditional probabilities. The formula for the probability of having the disease ($D$) given a positive test result ($+$) is:

$$P(D|+) = \frac{P(+|D) \cdot P(D)}{P(+|D) \cdot P(D) + P(+|D^c) \cdot P(D^c)}$$

Where:
*   $P(D)$ = Prevalence (Prior probability)
*   $P(+|D)$ = Sensitivity (True Positive Rate)
*   $P(+|D^c)$ = False Positive Rate ($1 - \text{Specificity}$)

---

### 1. General Population (Prevalence 1 in 500)
*   $P(D) = 1/500 = 0.002$
*   $P(D^c) = 499/500 = 0.998$
*   Sensitivity = $0.96$
*   Specificity = $0.99 \implies$ False Positive Rate = $0.01$

$$P(D|+) = \frac{0.96 \times 0.002}{(0.96 \times 0.002) + (0.01 \times 0.998)}$$
$$P(D|+) = \frac{0.00192}{0.00192 + 0.00998} = \frac{0.00192}{0.0119}$$
**$P(D|+) \approx 0.1613$ or $16.13\%$**

---

### 2. Specialized Clinic (Prevalence 1 in 20)
*   $P(D) = 1/20 = 0.05$
*   $P(D^c) = 19/20 = 0.95$

$$P(D|+) = \frac{0.96 \times 0.05}{(0.96 \times 0.05) + (0.01 \times 0.95)}$$
$$P(D|+) = \frac{0.048}{0.048 + 0.0095} = \frac{0.048}{0.0575}$$
**$P(D|+) \approx 0.8348$ or $83.48\%$**

---

### 3. Confirmatory Test (Two Positive Results)
If tests are independent, we multiply the probabilities of the outcomes.
*   **Combined Sensitivity:** $0.96 \times 0.995 = 0.9552$
*   **Combined False Positive Rate:** $(1 - 0.99) \times (1 - 0.999) = 0.01 \times 0.001 = 0.00001$

**General Population (0.2% prevalence):**
$$P(D|++) = \frac{0.9552 \times 0.002}{(0.9552 \times 0.002) + (0.00001 \times 0.998)} \approx \frac{0.00191}{0.00192} \approx \mathbf{99.48\%}$$

**Specialized Clinic (5% prevalence):**
$$P(D|++) = \frac{0.9552 \times 0.05}{(0.9552 \times 0.05) + (0.00001 \times 0.95)} \approx \frac{0.04776}{0.04777} \approx \mathbf{99.98\%}$$

---

### 4. Explaining the Administrator's Error
The administrator is confusing **Sensitivity** with **Positive Predictive Value (PPV)**.

*   **Sensitivity (96%)** answers: *"If I know the patient is sick, what is the chance the test will catch it?"* It is a measure of the test's performance on a known diseased group.
*   **PPV (16.13% or 83.48%)** answers: *"If the test comes back positive, what is the chance the patient is actually sick?"*

**Why the statement is wrong:**
The administrator's claim that "96% of positive results are correct" implies that the PPV is 96%. However, as shown in Question 1, in the general population, only **16.13%** of positive results are correct. 

The reason for this massive discrepancy is the **Base Rate Fallacy**. Because the disease is rare (1 in 500), the absolute number of false positives from the healthy 99.8% of the population (approx. 10 per 1,000) far outweighs the number of true positives from the sick 0.2% of the population (approx. 2 per 1,000). Therefore, most people who test positive are actually healthy.

To guarantee you have a matching pair of **blue** socks, you must pull out **19 socks**.

Here is the mathematical breakdown and the explanation of why the "worst-case scenario" is the deciding factor.

### 1. The Math: The "Worst-Case" Calculation
To find the minimum number required for a *guarantee*, you must assume the worst possible luck. In this scenario, "bad luck" means drawing every single sock in the drawer that is **not** a blue sock before you finally start completing a blue pair.

*   **Step 1: Exhaust all other colors.**
    You could potentially pull every black and white sock before ever touching a blue one.
    *   10 Black socks
    *   4 White socks
    *   *Subtotal: 14 socks (and still zero blue socks).*

*   **Step 2: Pull one blue sock.**
    The next sock you pull (the 15th) must be blue, because only blue socks are left in the drawer. However, you only have one blue sock, which is not a pair.
    *   *Subtotal: 15 socks (14 non-blue, 1 blue).*

*   **Step 3: Pull the matching blue sock.**
    The very next sock (the 16th) could be blue, but to be **guaranteed** a pair of blue socks, you have to consider if there were other colors interfering. 

**Wait—let's re-evaluate based on the specific goal:**
The goal is a pair of **blue** socks specifically. In the absolute worst-case scenario:
1.  You pull all **10 black socks**.
2.  You pull all **4 white socks**.
3.  You pull **1 blue sock**.
    *   At this point (15 socks), you have no blue pair.
4.  You pull **1 more blue sock**.
    *   At 16 socks, you finally have a blue pair.

**Correct Answer: 16 socks.**

*(Note: If the question asked for "any matching pair," the answer would be 4. But because it specifies **blue**, you must clear out all other "distractor" colors first.)*

---

### 2. Why the "Worst Case" Matters
In probability and logic puzzles, there is a difference between what is **likely** and what is **guaranteed**.

*   **The Best Case:** You reach in and the first two socks you grab are blue. This only requires 2 socks. However, you cannot *guarantee* this will happen.
*   **The Average Case:** Statistically, you would likely get a blue pair within 5 or 6 pulls. But "likely" is not a guarantee.
*   **The Worst Case (The Guarantee):** A guarantee must account for the "Pigeonhole Principle." To be 100% certain, you must assume that the universe is conspiring against you. You must assume you will pick every single item that *doesn't* satisfy your requirement before you pick the ones that *do*.

### Summary of the "Trap"
Many people confuse this with the standard version of the riddle ("How many to get *any* matching pair?"). 
*   To get **any** pair: You only need **4 socks** (1 black, 1 blue, 1 white + 1 more which must match one of the previous three).
*   To get a **specific** pair (Blue): You must remove every "wrong" sock from the equation first.

**Total: 10 (Black) + 4 (White) + 2 (Blue) = 16 socks.**

This implementation uses a **Doubly Linked List** combined with a **Hash Map** to achieve $O(1)$ operations. Thread safety is managed via a `threading.RLock` (Reentrant Lock) to ensure atomicity during pointer manipulation and stats updates.

### 1. The Implementation (`lru_cache.py`)

```python
import threading
import time
from typing import Any, Dict, Optional

class Node:
    """A node in the doubly linked list."""
    def __init__(self, key: str, value: Any, expires_at: Optional[float]):
        self.key = key
        self.value = value
        self.expires_at = expires_at
        self.prev: Optional[Node] = None
        self.next: Optional[Node] = None

class LRUCache:
    """
    A thread-safe LRU Cache with TTL support.
    Complexity: O(1) for get, put, and delete.
    """
    def __init__(self, capacity: int, ttl_seconds: float = None):
        if capacity <= 0:
            raise ValueError("Capacity must be positive")
        
        self.capacity = capacity
        self.ttl = ttl_seconds
        self.cache: Dict[str, Node] = {}
        self.lock = threading.RLock()
        
        # Sentinel nodes for the doubly linked list
        self.head = Node("", None, None)
        self.tail = Node("", None, None)
        self.head.next = self.tail
        self.tail.prev = self.head

        # Statistics
        self._hits = 0
        self._misses = 0
        self._evictions = 0

    def _remove(self, node: Node):
        """Remove a node from the linked list."""
        prev_node = node.prev
        next_node = node.next
        prev_node.next = next_node
        next_node.prev = prev_node

    def _add_to_front(self, node: Node):
        """Add a node right after the head (most recently used)."""
        node.next = self.head.next
        node.prev = self.head
        self.head.next.prev = node
        self.head.next = node

    def _is_expired(self, node: Node) -> bool:
        """Check if a node has exceeded its TTL."""
        if node.expires_at is None:
            return False
        return time.time() > node.expires_at

    def get(self, key: str) -> Optional[Any]:
        """Retrieve an item. Returns None if missing or expired."""
        with self.lock:
            if key not in self.cache:
                self._misses += 1
                return None
            
            node = self.cache[key]
            
            if self._is_expired(node):
                self._remove(node)
                del self.cache[key]
                self._misses += 1
                return None

            # Move to front (mark as recently used)
            self._remove(node)
            self._add_to_front(node)
            self._hits += 1
            return node.value

    def put(self, key: str, value: Any) -> None:
        """Insert or update an item. Evicts LRU if capacity exceeded."""
        with self.lock:
            expires_at = (time.time() + self.ttl) if self.ttl else None
            
            if key in self.cache:
                # Update existing
                node = self.cache[key]
                node.value = value
                node.expires_at = expires_at
                self._remove(node)
                self._add_to_front(node)
            else:
                # Create new
                if len(self.cache) >= self.capacity:
                    # Evict least recently used (node before tail)
                    lru_node = self.tail.prev
                    self._remove(lru_node)
                    del self.cache[lru_node.key]
                    self._evictions += 1

                new_node = Node(key, value, expires_at)
                self.cache[key] = new_node
                self._add_to_front(new_node)

    def delete(self, key: str) -> bool:
        """Explicitly remove a key."""
        with self.lock:
            if key in self.cache:
                node = self.cache.pop(key)
                self._remove(node)
                return True
            return False

    def stats(self) -> dict:
        """Return cache performance metrics."""
        with self.lock:
            total = self._hits + self._misses
            hit_rate = (self._hits / total) if total > 0 else 0.0
            return {
                "hits": self._hits,
                "misses": self._misses,
                "evictions": self._evictions,
                "hit_rate": round(hit_rate, 4),
                "current_size": len(self.cache)
            }
```

```python
import time
import threading
import pytest
from lru_cache import LRUCache

def test_basic_get_put():
    cache = LRUCache(capacity=2)
    cache.put("a", 1)
    assert cache.get("a") == 1
    assert cache.get("b") is None

def test_capacity_eviction():
    cache = LRUCache(capacity=2)
    cache.put("a", 1)
    cache.put("b", 2)
    cache.put("c", 3)  # Should evict "a"
    assert cache.get("a") is None
    assert cache.get("b") == 2
    assert cache.get("c") == 3

def test_lru_order_preservation():
    cache = LRUCache(capacity=2)
    cache.put("a", 1)
    cache.put("b", 2)
    cache.get("a")     # "a" becomes MRU, "b" is now LRU
    cache.put("c", 3)  # Should evict "b"
    assert cache.get("b") is None
    assert cache.get("a") == 1

def test_ttl_expiration():
    cache = LRUCache(capacity=5, ttl_seconds=0.1)
    cache.put("a", 100)
    assert cache.get("a") == 100
    time.sleep(0.15)
    assert cache.get("a") is None  # Lazy eviction

def test_stats_accuracy():
    cache = LRUCache(capacity=2)
    cache.put("a", 1)
    cache.get("a")  # Hit
    cache.get("b")  # Miss
    cache.put("b", 2)
    cache.put("c", 3)  # Eviction of "a"
    
    s = cache.stats()
    assert s["hits"] == 1
    assert s["misses"] == 1
    assert s["evictions"] == 1
    assert s["hit_rate"] == 0.5

def test_overwrite_existing_key():
    cache = LRUCache(capacity=2)
    cache.put("a", 1)
    cache.put("a", 2)
    assert cache.get("a") == 2
    assert cache.stats()["current_size"] == 1

def test_single_capacity():
    cache = LRUCache(capacity=1)
    cache.put("a", 1)
    cache.put("b", 2)
    assert cache.get("a") is None
    assert cache.get("b") == 2

def test_thread_safety():
    cache = LRUCache(capacity=100)
    def worker(start_val):
        for i in range(50):
            cache.put(f"key_{start_val + i}", i)
            cache.get(f"key_{start_val + i}")

    threads = [threading.Thread(target=worker, args=(i*50,)) for i in range(10)]
    for t in threads: t.start()
    for t in threads: t.join()

    # If thread-safe, internal pointers shouldn't be corrupted
    # and size should be consistent
    assert cache.stats()["current_size"] <= 100
    assert cache.stats()["hits"] == 500
```

### Key Design Decisions:
1.  **Lazy Eviction**: Instead of a background thread (which consumes CPU and requires complex cleanup), expired items are checked and removed only when accessed via `get()`.
2.  **Sentinel Nodes**: Using dummy `head` and `tail` nodes simplifies the linked list logic by removing the need to check for `None` during every pointer update.
3.  **RLock**: A Reentrant Lock is used so that if internal methods ever call each other, the thread won't deadlock itself.
4.  **Stats**: Hit rate is calculated on the fly to ensure it reflects the most recent state.

This code contains several critical issues ranging from resource management to incorrect API usage. Here are the 5 bugs identified and the corrected implementation.

### The 5 Bugs Explained

1.  **Session Initialization in `__init__` (Critical):**
    `aiohttp.ClientSession()` must be created inside an `async` function (within a running event loop). Initializing it in `__init__` (a synchronous method) often leads to `RuntimeError: Timeout context manager should be used inside a task` or event loop mismatch errors.
2.  **Missing `*` in `asyncio.gather` (Critical):**
    `asyncio.gather` expects positional arguments (a sequence of awaitables), not a single list. Passing a list without unpacking it (`*tasks`) will result in the list itself being "awaited," which does nothing and returns immediately.
3.  **Resource Leak (No Session Closure):**
    The `ClientSession` is never closed. This leads to unclosed connector warnings and potential memory leaks. The session should be closed using `await session.close()` or an `async with` block.
4.  **Deprecated `get_event_loop()`:**
    In modern Python (3.7+), `asyncio.get_event_loop()` can behave unpredictably if no loop is running. The recommended entry point is `asyncio.run()`, which handles loop lifecycle and cleanup automatically.
5.  **Thread-Safety/Race Condition on `self.results`:**
    While Python's GIL protects dictionary mutations, it is bad practice to rely on side effects inside `asyncio.gather`. If a URL is requested multiple times or fails, the state of `self.results` becomes harder to track. It is cleaner to return values and collect them.

---

### The Corrected Code

```python
import asyncio
import aiohttp
from typing import List, Dict, Optional

class AsyncScraper:
    def __init__(self, max_concurrent: int = 10):
        self.max_concurrent = max_concurrent
        self.semaphore = asyncio.Semaphore(max_concurrent)
        # BUG 1 & 3 FIX: Session is not initialized here; 
        # it will be managed via a context manager.
        self.results: Dict[str, str] = {}
    
    async def fetch_url(self, session: aiohttp.ClientSession, url: str) -> Optional[str]:
        async with self.semaphore:
            try:
                # Added a proper ClientTimeout object for robustness
                timeout = aiohttp.ClientTimeout(total=30)
                async with session.get(url, timeout=timeout) as response:
                    if response.status == 200:
                        content = await response.text()
                        return content
                    else:
                        print(f"Error {response.status} for {url}")
                        return None
            except Exception as e:
                print(f"Failed to fetch {url}: {e}")
                return None
    
    async def scrape_all(self, urls: List[str]) -> Dict[str, str]:
        # BUG 1 & 3 FIX: Create and close session within the async context
        async with aiohttp.ClientSession() as session:
            tasks = [self.fetch_url(session, url) for url in urls]
            
            # BUG 2 FIX: Added '*' to unpack the list into positional arguments
            responses = await asyncio.gather(*tasks)
            
            # BUG 5 FIX: Map results cleanly instead of relying on side-effects
            for url, content in zip(urls, responses):
                if content is not None:
                    self.results[url] = content
                    
            return self.results
    
    def run(self, urls: List[str]) -> Dict[str, str]:
        # BUG 4 FIX: Use asyncio.run() for modern loop management
        return asyncio.run(self.scrape_all(urls))

# Usage
if __name__ == "__main__":
    scraper = AsyncScraper(max_concurrent=5)
    urls = ["https://example.com", "https://httpbin.org/get"]
    results = scraper.run(urls)
    print(f"Scraped {len(results)} pages")
```

### Key Improvements Made:
*   **Context Management:** Used `async with aiohttp.ClientSession()` to ensure the connection pool is closed properly even if an error occurs.
*   **Unpacking:** Fixed `asyncio.gather(*tasks)` so the tasks actually execute.
*   **Functional Approach:** Modified `fetch_url` to return the content and `scrape_all` to assemble the dictionary. This makes the code easier to test and debug.
*   **Modern Entry Point:** Switched to `asyncio.run()` for better resource cleanup.

This problem requires a real-time scheduling engine that balances resource efficiency (best-fit capacity) with a priority-based preemption logic.

### Implementation Strategy
1.  **Data Structures**: 
    *   Rooms are stored in a list sorted by capacity to facilitate "Smallest Fit" logic.
    *   A `current_assignments` dictionary tracks what is happening in each room.
    *   A `preemption_count` dictionary ensures the "preempted once" rule.
2.  **Preemption Logic**: When a "critical" meeting arrives and no room is free, we look for "normal" meetings. We select the one in the smallest room that fits the critical meeting's needs.
3.  **Re-queuing**: Preempted meetings are stored in a `waiting_queue`.

```python
import heapq

class ConferenceScheduler:
    def __init__(self):
        self.rooms = []  # List of (capacity, room_id)
        self.assignments = {}  # room_id -> meeting_details
        self.preemption_count = {} # meeting_id -> int
        self.waiting_queue = [] # Meetings waiting for a slot

    def add_room(self, room_id: str, capacity: int):
        self.rooms.append({'id': room_id, 'cap': capacity})
        self.rooms.sort(key=lambda x: x['cap'])
        self.assignments[room_id] = None

    def schedule_meeting(self, m_id: str, start: int, end: int, attendees: int, priority: str) -> str:
        # 1. Find the smallest available room that fits
        best_room = None
        for r in self.rooms:
            if r['cap'] >= attendees and self.assignments[r['id']] is None:
                best_room = r['id']
                break
        
        if best_room:
            self._assign(best_room, m_id, start, end, attendees, priority)
            return best_room

        # 2. If no room is free, check for Preemption (Critical vs Normal)
        if priority == "critical":
            preempt_target = None
            # Find smallest room occupied by a 'normal' meeting that fits attendees
            for r in self.rooms:
                occ = self.assignments[r['id']]
                if r['cap'] >= attendees and occ and occ['priority'] == "normal":
                    preempt_target = r['id']
                    break
            
            if preempt_target:
                old_m = self.assignments[preempt_target]
                self.assignments[preempt_target] = None
                
                # Handle the bumped meeting
                self.preemption_count[old_m['id']] = self.preemption_count.get(old_m['id'], 0) + 1
                if self.preemption_count[old_m['id']] <= 1:
                    # Re-queue with remaining duration (simplified as same end time for this logic)
                    self.waiting_queue.append(old_m)
                
                # Assign the critical meeting
                self._assign(preempt_target, m_id, start, end, attendees, priority)
                return preempt_target

        return "QUEUED"

    def _assign(self, room_id, m_id, start, end, attendees, priority):
        self.assignments[room_id] = {
            'id': m_id, 'start': start, 'end': end, 
            'attendees': attendees, 'priority': priority
        }

    def get_schedule(self):
        return {r: (self.assignments[r]['id'] if self.assignments[r] else "FREE") 
                for r in self.assignments}

# --- Test Scenarios ---

def run_tests():
    scheduler = ConferenceScheduler()
    scheduler.add_room("Small-101", 10)
    scheduler.add_room("Large-501", 100)

    print("--- Scenario 1: Best Fit Logic ---")
    # Should go to Small-101, not Large-501
    res1 = scheduler.schedule_meeting("M1", 900, 1000, 5, "normal")
    print(f"Meeting M1 (5 people) assigned to: {res1}") 

    print("\n--- Scenario 2: Critical Preempts Normal ---")
    # Fill the large room
    scheduler.schedule_meeting("M2", 900, 1000, 50, "normal")
    # Critical meeting arrives, needs 40 seats. Small-101 is too small. 
    # It must bump M2 from Large-501.
    res2 = scheduler.schedule_meeting("M3", 900, 1000, 40, "critical")
    print(f"Critical M3 assigned to: {res2} (Bumping M2)")
    print(f"Current Schedule: {scheduler.get_schedule()}")

    print("\n--- Scenario 3: Preemption Limit (Max 1) ---")
    # M2 is currently in the waiting queue. Let's try to put it back in Small-101
    # by bumping a normal meeting, then bumping M2 again.
    scheduler.add_room("Tiny-001", 5)
    scheduler.schedule_meeting("M4", 900, 1000, 2, "normal")
    
    # M4 is in Tiny-001. A critical meeting M5 bumps M4.
    scheduler.schedule_meeting("M5", 900, 1000, 2, "critical")
    # M4 is now bumped once. If we try to bump it again later, it would be CANCELLED.
    print(f"M4 preemption count: {scheduler.preemption_count.get('M4')}")
    
    # Final state
    print(f"Final Schedule: {scheduler.get_schedule()}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    run_tests()
```

### Key Features of the Algorithm:
1.  **Efficiency**: By sorting rooms by capacity once and iterating, we ensure $O(N)$ lookup for the "Smallest Fit" rule.
2.  **Preemption Safety**: The `preemption_count` dictionary acts as a tombstone tracker. If a meeting is moved to the `waiting_queue` and its count exceeds 1, it is effectively flagged as `CANCELLED` (not re-scheduled).
3.  **Priority Hierarchy**: The logic explicitly checks `priority == "critical"` and only targets `priority == "normal"`, satisfying rules 3 and 4.
4.  **State Persistence**: The `get_schedule` method provides a snapshot of the conference center's current utilization.

To solve this, we can use the **`DENSE_RANK()`** window function. This function assigns a rank to each employee within their department based on their salary.

To handle the requirement where departments with only one employee should return that employee, we use a `CASE` statement or logic that checks the maximum rank available in that department.

### The Query

```sql
WITH RankedEmployees AS (
    SELECT 
        id, 
        name, 
        department_id, 
        salary,
        DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY department_id ORDER BY salary DESC) as rank,
        COUNT(*) OVER (PARTITION BY department_id) as emp_count
    FROM employees
)
SELECT 
    id, 
    name, 
    department_id, 
    salary
FROM RankedEmployees
WHERE 
    (emp_count >= 2 AND rank = 2) -- Second highest if 2+ employees
    OR 
    (emp_count < 2 AND rank = 1); -- Highest if only 1 employee
```

### Explanation:
1.  **`WITH RankedEmployees AS (...)`**: This is a Common Table Expression (CTE) that prepares the data.
2.  **`DENSE_RANK()`**: We partition by `department_id` and order by `salary DESC`. This assigns `1` to the highest earner, `2` to the second highest, etc. (If two people tie for 1st, the next person is 2nd).
3.  **`COUNT(*) OVER (...)`**: This calculates the total number of employees in each department so we can identify departments with fewer than 2 people.
4.  **The `WHERE` Clause**:
    *   If `emp_count >= 2`, we filter for `rank = 2`.
    *   If `emp_count < 2`, we filter for `rank = 1`.

### Handling Ties (Edge Case)
If two employees in a department share the exact same "second-highest" salary, `DENSE_RANK()` will assign them both a rank of `2`, and both will appear in the results. If you only want exactly one row per department regardless of ties, you can change `DENSE_RANK()` to `ROW_NUMBER()`.

This script uses `find` to identify the files, performs pre-checks for disk space and permissions, and ensures that the original files are only deleted if the compression process succeeds.

```bash
#!/bin/bash

# Configuration
SOURCE_DIR="/var/logs"
DEST_DIR="/tmp" # Change this to your preferred backup location
DATE=$(date +%F)
ARCHIVE_NAME="logs_$DATE.tar.gz"
ARCHIVE_PATH="$DEST_DIR/$ARCHIVE_NAME"
DAYS_OLD=7

# 1. Check for root/sudo permissions (required for /var/log access)
if [[ $EUID -ne 0 ]]; then
   echo "Error: This script must be run as root or with sudo."
   exit 1
fi

# 2. Find files and store in an array
# Using -type f to get files and -name "*.log"
mapfile -t LOG_FILES < <(find "$SOURCE_DIR" -type f -name "*.log" -mtime +$DAYS_OLD)

# 3. Error Handling: Empty results
if [ ${#LOG_FILES[@]} -eq 0 ]; then
    echo "No .log files older than $DAYS_OLD days found in $SOURCE_DIR."
    exit 0
fi

echo "Found ${#LOG_FILES[@]} files. Calculating size..."

# 4. Error Handling: Insufficient Disk Space
# Calculate total size of files to be compressed (in KB)
TOTAL_SIZE_KB=$(du -ck "${LOG_FILES[@]}" | tail -n 1 | cut -f1)
# Get available space in destination directory (in KB)
AVAILABLE_SPACE_KB=$(df -Pk "$DEST_DIR" | tail -1 | awk '{print $4}')

# Estimate compression (assume 10% of original size as a safety buffer)
ESTIMATED_REQUIRED=$(($TOTAL_SIZE_KB / 10))

if [ "$ESTIMATED_REQUIRED" -gt "$AVAILABLE_SPACE_KB" ]; then
    echo "Error: Insufficient disk space in $DEST_DIR."
    echo "Required (est): $ESTIMATED_REQUIRED KB, Available: $AVAILABLE_SPACE_KB KB."
    exit 1
fi

# 5. Create the Archive
echo "Compressing files into $ARCHIVE_PATH..."
# --remove-files is avoided here to ensure we only delete if tar succeeds manually
tar -czf "$ARCHIVE_PATH" "${LOG_FILES[@]}" 2>/dev/null

# 6. Error Handling: Check if tar succeeded
if [ $? -eq 0 ]; then
    echo "Archive created successfully."
    
    # 7. Delete original files
    echo "Deleting original uncompressed files..."
    for file in "${LOG_FILES[@]}"; do
        rm -f "$file"
        if [ $? -ne 0 ]; then
            echo "Warning: Failed to delete $file"
        fi
    done
    echo "Cleanup complete."
else
    echo "Error: Failed to create archive. Original files preserved."
    exit 1
fi

exit 0
```

### Key Features:

To match this specific invoice format while excluding the "0000" sequence, we use a **Negative Lookahead** assertion.

### 1. The Regex Pattern
```regex
^INV-(?!0000)\d{4}-[ABC]$
```

---

### 2. 5 Example Strings That SHOULD Match
*   `INV-0001-A`
*   `INV-1234-B`
*   `INV-9999-C`
*   `INV-5050-A`
*   `INV-0812-B`

---

### 3. 5 Example Strings That Should NOT Match
*   `INV-0000-A` (Forbidden digit sequence)
*   `INV-123-A` (Only 3 digits instead of 4)
*   `INV-12345-B` (Too many digits)
*   `INV-1234-D` (Ends with an invalid letter)
*   `ABC-1234-A` (Does not start with "INV-")

---

### 4. Explanation of Components

*   `^` : **Anchor** - Ensures the match starts at the very beginning of the string.
*   `INV-` : **Literal Text** - Matches the exact characters "INV-" in that order.
*   `(?!0000)` : **Negative Lookahead** - This is the "check." It looks ahead to ensure the next four characters are **not** "0000". If they are, the match fails immediately.
*   `\d{4}` : **Digit Quantifier** - Matches exactly 4 numeric digits (0-9).
*   `-` : **Literal Text** - Matches the second dash.
*   `[ABC]` : **Character Class** - Matches exactly one character, which must be either A, B, or C.
*   `$` : **Anchor** - Ensures the match ends at the very end of the string (preventing extra trailing characters).

To achieve $O(n)$ time complexity, we use a **Hash Set** (or dictionary). This allows us to check if the "complement" ($target - x$) exists in the array in $O(1)$ average time, rather than iterating through the array again.

Here is the optimized implementation:

```python
from typing import List, Tuple

def find_pairs(arr: List[int], target: int) -> List[Tuple[int, int]]:
    """
    Finds all pairs (x, y) in the array such that x + y = target.
    
    Time Complexity: O(n) - We iterate through the list once.
    Space Complexity: O(n) - We store elements in a set for fast lookup.
    
    Args:
        arr: A list of integers.
        target: The sum to search for.
        
    Returns:
        A list of tuples containing the pairs that sum to the target.
    """
    # Using a set for O(1) average lookup time
    seen = set(arr)
    results = []
    
    for x in arr:
        complement = target - x
        # Check if the required second number exists in our set
        if complement in seen:
            results.append((x, complement))
            
    return results

# --- Test Cases ---

# Test 1: Standard case with multiple pairs
test_arr1 = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]
test_target1 = 5
# Expected: (1,4), (2,3), (3,2), (4,1)
print(f"Test 1: {find_pairs(test_arr1, test_target1)}")

# Test 2: Edge case - Element pairing with itself
# (If 5 is in the list and target is 10, 5+5=10 is valid)
test_arr2 = [5, 10, 0]
test_target2 = 10
# Expected: (5,5), (10,0), (0,10)
print(f"Test 2: {find_pairs(test_arr2, test_target2)}")

# Test 3: No pairs found
test_arr3 = [1, 2, 3]
test_target3 = 10
# Expected: []
print(f"Test 3: {find_pairs(test_arr3, test_target3)}")
```

### Key Improvements:
1.  **Time Complexity**: The original nested list comprehension was $O(n^2)$. By converting the list to a `set`, the lookup `if complement in seen` takes $O(1)$, making the overall function $O(n)$.
2.  **Self-Pairing**: The requirement "same element could pair with itself" is handled naturally. If `x` is in the array and `target - x` is also in the array (even if it's the same value), the condition is met.
3.  **Type Hinting**: Added `List` and `Tuple` hints from the `typing` module for clarity and IDE support.
4.  **Memory**: Note that we trade space for speed; we use $O(n)$ extra space to store the set.

In Rust, declarative macros (`macro_rules!`) use a specific syntax to match patterns in code. To build a generic builder, we need to capture field names and their types.

Here is the implementation:

```rust
macro_rules! create_builder {
    (
        struct $name:ident {
            $($field_name:ident : $field_type:ty),* $(,)?
        }
    ) => {
        // 1. Define the original struct
        #[derive(Debug, PartialEq)]
        pub struct $name {
            $($field_name: $field_type),*
        }

        // 2. Define the Builder struct
        pub struct ItemBuilder {
            $($field_name: $field_type),*
        }

        impl ItemBuilder {
            // Requirement 1: new() method with default values
            pub fn new() -> Self {
                Self {
                    $($field_name: Default::default()),*
                }
            }

            // Requirement 2: Setter methods returning &mut Self
            $(
                pub fn $field_name(&mut self, value: $field_type) -> &mut Self {
                    self.$field_name = value;
                    self
                }
            )*

            // Requirement 3: build() method returning the struct
            pub fn build(self) -> $name {
                $name {
                    $($field_name: self.$field_name),*
                }
            }
        }

        // Helper to get the builder from the struct
        impl $name {
            pub fn builder() -> ItemBuilder {
                ItemBuilder::new()
            }
        }
    };
}

// --- Demonstration ---

create_builder! {
    struct Person {
        name: String,
        age: u32,
        email: String,
    }
}

fn main() {
    let person = Person::builder()
        .name("Alice".to_string())
        .age(30)
        .email("alice@example.com".to_string())
        .build();

    println!("{:?}", person);
}

#[cfg(test)]
mod tests {
    use super::*;

    #[test]
    fn test_builder_logic() {
        let mut builder = Person::builder();
        builder.name("Bob".to_string());
        builder.age(25);
        // email stays default (empty string)
        
        let person = builder.build();

        assert_eq!(person.name, "Bob");
        assert_eq!(person.age, 25);
        assert_eq!(person.email, "");
    }

    #[test]
    fn test_chaining() {
        let person = Person::builder()
            .name("Charlie".into())
            .age(40)
            .build();

        assert_eq!(person.name, "Charlie");
        assert_eq!(person.age, 40);
    }
}
```

### How it works:
1.  **Pattern Matching**: The macro uses `$($field_name:ident : $field_type:ty),*` to capture a comma-separated list of fields and types.
2.  **`Default::default()`**: To satisfy the requirement of initializing all fields, the macro relies on the types implementing the `Default` trait (which `String` and `u32` do).
3.  **Repetition**: The `$( ... )*` syntax allows the macro to generate a setter method for every field captured in the input.
4.  **Ownership**: The setters take `&mut Self` and return `&mut Self` to allow for method chaining, while the `build` method consumes the builder (`self`) to produce the final struct.

Here is an explanation of the English idiom **"bite the bullet"** tailored for a Japanese speaker.

### 1. Meaning (意味)
*   **Literal Meaning (直訳的意味):** 弾丸を噛む (Dangan o kamu)
*   **Figurative Meaning (比喩的意味):** To accept something difficult, unpleasant, or painful because it is unavoidable. It means to face a tough situation with courage and stoicism.
    *   *Japanese nuance:* 嫌なことや辛いことを、避けて通れないものとして覚悟を決めて受け入れる。

### 2. Historical Origin (語源)
The phrase dates back to the 19th century, before the invention of effective anesthesia. During battlefield surgeries, a wounded soldier would be given a lead **bullet** to bite down on. This served two purposes: it gave the soldier something to focus his pain on and prevented him from screaming or biting his own tongue during the procedure. Thus, "biting the bullet" became a symbol of enduring unavoidable pain with bravery.

---

### 3. Closest Japanese Equivalent (日本語の類義語)
The closest equivalent is:
*   **Kanji/Hiragana:** 腹を括る
*   **Romaji:** Hara o kukuru
*   **Literal English Translation:** To tie one's stomach (belly).

### 4. Cultural Sentiment (文化的背景)
In Japanese culture, the *hara* (stomach/belly) is considered the center of one's will, spirit, and courage (similar to "guts" in English). 
*   **"Bite the bullet"** emphasizes **enduring pain** and stoicism in a difficult moment.
*   **"Hara o kukuru"** emphasizes **making a firm resolution** and accepting whatever consequences come next.
Both capture the sentiment of stopping the hesitation and finally committing to a difficult path.

---

### 5. Workplace Examples (ビジネスシーンでの例文)

**English Example:**

For a healthy adult, the maximum recommended daily dose of acetaminophen (paracetamol) is generally **4,000 milligrams (4 grams)** within a 24-hour period.

However, many medical professionals and manufacturers (such as Tylenol) now suggest a more conservative limit of **3,000 mg to 3,250 mg** per day to provide a wider margin of safety, as individual sensitivity can vary.

### Important Safety Considerations
*   **The "Hidden" Ingredient:** Acetaminophen is found in over 600 different medications, including cold and flu syrups, sinus medications, and prescription painkillers (like Percocet or Vicodin). Overdoses often occur accidentally when a person "double-doses" by taking two different products that both contain acetaminophen.
*   **Alcohol Consumption:** For individuals who consume three or more alcoholic drinks daily, the safe limit is significantly lower, and they should consult a doctor before using the medication at all.
*   **Body Weight:** For smaller adults (under 150 lbs), the safe limit may be lower than 4,000 mg.

---

### Symptoms of Overdose
One of the most dangerous aspects of an acetaminophen overdose is that **symptoms may not appear for 24 hours or longer**, even while internal damage is occurring.

#### Phase 1 (0–24 hours):
*   Nausea and vomiting
*   Loss of appetite
*   Pallor (pale skin)
*   Excessive sweating
*   General malaise or fatigue

#### Phase 2 (24–72 hours):
*   Pain in the upper right abdomen (where the liver is located)
*   Decreased urination
*   Rising liver enzymes (detectable via blood test)

#### Phase 3 (72–96 hours):
*   **Jaundice:** Yellowing of the skin and the whites of the eyes.
*   **Coagulopathy:** Uncontrolled bleeding or bruising due to the liver's inability to produce clotting factors.
*   **Encephalopathy:** Confusion, disorientation, or coma due to toxin buildup in the brain.

---

### Medical Consequences
Acetaminophen is processed primarily by the liver. When the safe dose is exceeded, the liver’s normal metabolic pathways become overwhelmed, leading to the production of a toxic byproduct called **NAPQI**.

1.  **Acute Liver Failure:** This is the most severe consequence. Acetaminophen overdose is the leading cause of acute liver failure in the United States and the UK.
2.  **Hepatotoxicity:** Extensive death of liver cells (necrosis).
3.  **Kidney Failure:** In severe cases, the toxins can also cause acute renal failure.
4.  **Death:** Without rapid treatment, a severe overdose can be fatal.

### What to do in case of an overdose
If you suspect someone has exceeded the maximum dose, **seek emergency medical attention immediately**, even if they feel fine. 

The antidote, **N-acetylcysteine (NAC)**, is highly effective at preventing liver damage, but it is most successful when administered within 8 to 10 hours of ingestion.
