In this inverted universe, the thermal properties of water are flipped: **0°C is the boiling point** and **100°C is the freezing point.**

To understand what happens to your 50°C glass of water in a -10°C room, we must first establish the "phase map" for this universe:
*   **Above 0°C:** Water is a gas (steam).
*   **Between 0°C and 100°C:** Water is a liquid.
*   **Below 100°C:** Water is a solid (ice).

*Wait—there is a logical paradox here.* In a universe where water boils at 0°C and freezes at 100°C, the liquid phase exists only between 0°C and 100°C. However, since 100°C is the freezing point and 0°C is the boiling point, the liquid phase is "trapped" in a state where it is colder than its freezing point but hotter than its boiling point. 

Assuming the laws of thermodynamics still apply (heat flows from hot to cold), here is the step-by-step progression of your 50°C glass of water:

### 1. The Initial State (50°C)
At 50°C, your water is currently in the **liquid phase**. It is perfectly stable because it is between the boiling point (0°C) and the freezing point (100°C).

### 2. The Cooling Process (50°C down to 0°C)
You place the glass in a -10°C room. Heat begins to leave the water and enter the room. As the temperature drops from 50°C toward 0°C, the water remains a liquid. It is getting closer and closer to its **boiling point**.

### 3. Reaching the Boiling Point (0°C)
As the water hits 0°C, it reaches its boiling point. In our universe, water absorbs energy to boil; in this universe, the water is *losing* energy to the room. 
*   **The Phase Change:** As the water attempts to drop below 0°C, it undergoes a phase change from liquid to gas (steam). 
*   **The Result:** The water will rapidly evaporate/boil away into the room as steam. Because the room is -10°C, this steam will immediately lose its kinetic energy.

### 4. The Transition of the Steam
As the steam enters the -10°C room, it is now significantly hotter than its surroundings. It will rapidly lose heat. 
*   The steam will cool down from 0°C toward -10°C. 
*   However, it must pass through the "liquid" range (0°C to 100°C) and eventually hit the **freezing point (100°C)**. 

### 5. The Final State (The Paradox)
This is where the physics of your universe becomes chaotic. As the steam cools, it will eventually reach the 100°C mark. But wait—in this universe, 100°C is the **freezing point**. 

As the steam cools down toward the room temperature of -10°C, it will hit 100°C and instantly undergo **deposition** (turning directly from gas to solid ice). 

### Summary of the sequence:
1.  **50°C to 0°C:** The water remains a liquid, cooling down.
2.  **At 0°C:** The liquid boils, turning into steam.
3.  **0°C to 100°C:** The steam exists briefly as it cools.
4.  **At 100°C:** The steam hits the freezing point and turns into solid ice crystals.
5.  **100°C to -10°C:** The ice crystals continue to cool until they reach the ambient temperature of the room (-10°C).

**Final Result:** You started with a glass of liquid water and ended with a glass containing a pile of very cold ice crystals.

To solve these problems, we use Bayes' Theorem. Let $D$ be the event of having the disease, and $+$ be the event of testing positive.

**Definitions:**
*   $P(D)$: Prevalence (Prior probability)
*   $P(+|D)$: Sensitivity (0.96)
*   $P(-|D^c)$: Specificity (0.99) $\rightarrow P(+|D^c) = 1 - 0.99 = 0.01$ (False Positive Rate)

---

### 1. General Population (Prevalence = 1/500 = 0.002)
We want to find $P(D|+)$, the Positive Predictive Value (PPV).

$$P(D|+) = \frac{P(+|D)P(D)}{P(+|D)P(D) + P(+|D^c)P(D^c)}$$
$$P(D|+) = \frac{(0.96)(0.002)}{(0.96)(0.002) + (0.01)(0.998)} = \frac{0.00192}{0.00192 + 0.00998} = \frac{0.00192}{0.0119} \approx \mathbf{16.13\%}$$

---

### 2. Specialized Clinic (Prevalence = 1/20 = 0.05)
$$P(D|+) = \frac{(0.96)(0.05)}{(0.96)(0.05) + (0.01)(0.95)} = \frac{0.048}{0.048 + 0.0095} = \frac{0.048}{0.0575} \approx \mathbf{83.48\%}$$

---

### 3. Confirmatory Test (Sensitivity = 0.995, Specificity = 0.999)
If tests are independent, the combined probability of testing positive on both is:
*   $P(++|D) = 0.96 \times 0.995 = 0.9552$
*   $P(++|D^c) = 0.01 \times 0.001 = 0.00001$

**General Population (0.002):**
$$P(D|++) = \frac{0.9552 \times 0.002}{(0.9552 \times 0.002) + (0.00001 \times 0.998)} = \frac{0.0019104}{0.0019104 + 0.00000998} \approx \mathbf{99.48\%}$$

**Specialized Clinic (0.05):**
$$P(D|++) = \frac{0.9552 \times 0.05}{(0.9552 \times 0.05) + (0.00001 \times 0.95)} = \frac{0.04776}{0.04776 + 0.0000095} \approx \mathbf{99.98\%}$$

---

### 4. Addressing the Administrator's Statement
The administrator is confusing **Sensitivity** with **Positive Predictive Value (PPV)**.

*   **Sensitivity** is a property of the *test*: it measures the probability that the test will correctly identify someone who *already has* the disease.
*   **PPV** is a property of the *test result in a specific population*: it measures the probability that someone who *tests positive* actually has the disease.

**Why the statement is wrong:**
In the general population, even though the test is 96% sensitive, the PPV is only **16.13%**. This happens because the disease is rare (low prevalence). When the disease is rare, the absolute number of "false positives" generated by the 1% error rate (from the large healthy population) significantly outweighs the number of "true positives" generated by the 96% sensitivity (from the tiny diseased population). Therefore, a positive result is more likely to be a false alarm than a true diagnosis.

To guarantee you have at least one matching pair of **blue** socks, you must pull out **16 socks**.

### The Math: The "Worst-Case Scenario"
In probability and logic puzzles of this type, a "guarantee" requires you to account for the **worst-case scenario**. This means you must assume you are the unluckiest person possible and that you pull out every single sock that is *not* a blue pair before finally pulling the second blue sock.

Here is the step-by-step breakdown of the worst-case sequence:

1.  **Pull all the non-blue socks first:**
    *   There are 10 black socks and 4 white socks.
    *   If you pull all of these, you have **14 socks** in your hand, but zero blue socks.
2.  **Pull the first blue sock:**
    *   You pull one more sock (the 15th). It is guaranteed to be blue because all the black and white socks are already out of the drawer.
    *   You now have 14 non-blue socks and 1 blue sock. You still do not have a matching pair of blue socks.
3.  **Pull the second blue sock:**
    *   You pull one more sock (the 16th). This must be blue.
    *   You now have a matching pair of blue socks.

**Calculation:** 10 (black) + 4 (white) + 2 (blue) = **16 socks.**

### Why the "Worst Case" Matters

To implement a thread-safe LRU cache with TTL, we use an `OrderedDict` (which maintains insertion order) combined with a `threading.RLock` for concurrency control.

### Implementation

```python
import threading
import time
from collections import OrderedDict
from typing import Any, Optional, Dict

class LRUCache:
    """Thread-safe LRU Cache with optional TTL."""

    def __init__(self, capacity: int, ttl_seconds: float = None):
        if capacity <= 0:
            raise ValueError("Capacity must be > 0")
        self.capacity = capacity
        self.ttl = ttl_seconds
        self.cache: OrderedDict = OrderedDict()
        self.lock = threading.RLock()
        
        self.hits = 0
        self.misses = 0
        self.evictions = 0

    def _is_expired(self, entry: dict) -> bool:
        if self.ttl is None:
            return False
        return (time.time() - entry['timestamp']) > self.ttl

    def get(self, key: str) -> Optional[Any]:
        with self.lock:
            if key not in self.cache:
                self.misses += 1
                return None
            
            entry = self.cache[key]
            if self._is_expired(entry):
                self.delete(key)
                self.misses += 1
                return None
            
            self.cache.move_to_end(key)
            self.hits += 1
            return entry['value']

    def put(self, key: str, value: Any) -> None:
        with self.lock:
            if key in self.cache:
                self.cache.move_to_end(key)
            
            self.cache[key] = {'value': value, 'timestamp': time.time()}
            
            if len(self.cache) > self.capacity:
                self.cache.popitem(last=False)
                self.evictions += 1

    def delete(self, key: str) -> bool:
        with self.lock:
            if key in self.cache:
                self.cache.pop(key)
                return True
            return False

    def stats(self) -> dict:
        with self.lock:
            total = self.hits + self.misses
            hit_rate = self.hits / total if total > 0 else 0.0
            return {
                "hits": self.hits,
                "misses": self.misses,
                "evictions": self.evictions,
                "hit_rate": hit_rate
            }
```

To solve the "Meeting Room Tetris" problem, we need to maintain the state of rooms, track active meetings, and manage a queue for preempted or unassigned meetings.

### Implementation Strategy
1.  **Room Management**: Store rooms in a sorted list by capacity to efficiently find the "smallest fit."
2.  **Preemption Logic**: When a "critical" meeting arrives, we scan active meetings. If we find a "normal" meeting in a room that fits the critical request, we bump it.
3.  **State Tracking**: We track `preemption_count` per meeting to enforce the "only once" rule.

```python
import heapq

class ConferenceScheduler:
    def __init__(self):
        self.rooms = []  # List of [capacity, room_id]
        self.active_meetings = {}  # room_id: meeting_data
        self.meeting_history = {}  # meeting_id: {..., 'preempted': bool}

    def add_room(self, room_id: str, capacity: int):
        self.rooms.append({'id': room_id, 'capacity': capacity})
        self.rooms.sort(key=lambda x: x['capacity'])

    def schedule_meeting(self, m_id, start, end, attendees, priority):
        # 1. Try to find an empty room
        best_room = self._find_best_room(attendees)
        
        if best_room:
            self._assign(m_id, best_room, start, end, attendees, priority)
            return best_room['id']

        # 2. If critical, try to preempt a normal meeting
        if priority == "critical":
            preemptible = self._find_preemptible_room(attendees)
            if preemptible:
                bumped = self.active_meetings.pop(preemptible['id'])
                self._handle_bumped_meeting(bumped)
                self._assign(m_id, preemptible, start, end, attendees, priority)
                return preemptible['id']

        return "QUEUED"

    def _find_best_room(self, attendees):
        for room in self.rooms:
            if room['capacity'] >= attendees and room['id'] not in self.active_meetings:
                return room
        return None

    def _find_preemptible_room(self, attendees):
        for room in self.rooms:
            if room['capacity'] >= attendees and room['id'] in self.active_meetings:
                m = self.active_meetings[room['id']]
                if m['priority'] == "normal":
                    return room
        return None

    def _assign(self, m_id, room, start, end, attendees, priority):
        self.active_meetings[room['id']] = {
            'm_id': m_id, 'start': start, 'end': end, 
            'attendees': attendees, 'priority': priority,
            'preempted': self.meeting_history.get(m_id, {}).get('preempted', False)
        }
        self.meeting_history[m_id] = self.active_meetings[room['id']]

    def _handle_bumped_meeting(self, meeting):
        if meeting['preempted']:
            meeting['status'] = "CANCELLED"
        else:
            meeting['preempted'] = True
            meeting['status'] = "QUEUED"

    def get_schedule(self):
        return {r_id: m['m_id'] for r_id, m in self.active_meetings.items()}

scheduler.add_room("Small", 10)
scheduler.add_room("Large", 50)

# Expected: Small

# Expected: Small (M1 bumped to QUEUED)
```

*   **Efficiency**: Rooms are sorted by capacity, ensuring $O(N)$ search for the smallest fit.
*   **Preemption**: The `_find_preemptible_room` method specifically targets "normal" meetings, respecting the rule that "high" or "critical" meetings cannot be bumped.
*   **State Integrity**: The `meeting_history` dictionary acts as a persistent record to track if a meeting has already been preempted, satisfying the "no more than once" rule.

To achieve this, we can use the `DENSE_RANK()` window function to rank employees by salary within each department. 

We then use a `CASE` statement to handle the logic: if the rank is 2, we select that employee; if the rank is 1 and there is no rank 2 (or simply as a fallback), we select the top earner.

```sql
WITH RankedEmployees AS (
    SELECT 
        id, 
        name, 
        department_id, 
        salary,
        DENSE_RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY department_id ORDER BY salary DESC) as rnk,
        COUNT(*) OVER (PARTITION BY department_id) as dept_count
    FROM employees
)
SELECT 
    id, 
    name, 
    department_id, 
    salary
FROM RankedEmployees
WHERE 
    -- If department has < 2 employees, pick the highest (rank 1)
    (dept_count < 2 AND rnk = 1)
    -- Otherwise, pick the second highest (rank 2)
    OR (dept_count >= 2 AND rnk = 2);
```

### Explanation:
1.  **`DENSE_RANK()`**: Assigns a rank to each employee based on their salary within their department (`PARTITION BY department_id`). We use `DENSE_RANK` instead of `ROW_NUMBER` so that if two people tie for the highest salary, they are both considered rank 1, and the next distinct salary is rank 2.
2.  **`COUNT(*) OVER(...)`**: This window function calculates the total number of employees in each department without collapsing the rows, allowing us to check the "less than 2 employees" condition.
3.  **`WHERE` Clause**:
    *   If the department size is less than 2, we filter for `rnk = 1`.
    *   If the department size is 2 or more, we filter for `rnk = 2`.

**Note:** If a department has 2 or more employees but they all have the exact same salary, `DENSE_RANK` will only return rank 1. In that scenario, the query will return nothing for that department.     exit 0

```bash
    if [ ! -r "$file" ]; then
        echo "Error: Permission denied reading $file. Skipping."
        # Remove from list so we don't try to archive/delete it

REQUIRED_SPACE=$(du -ck $(cat "$TEMP_FILE_LIST") | tail -n1 | cut -f1)
AVAILABLE_SPACE=$(df -Pk "$DEST_DIR" | tail -n1 | awk '{print $4}')

    echo "Error: Insufficient disk space to create archive."
    exit 1
fi
```

To achieve $O(n)$ time complexity, we use a hash map (dictionary) to store the frequency of each number encountered. This allows us to check for the existence of the "complement" (`target - x`) in constant time.

### The $O(n)$ Implementation

```python
from typing import List, Tuple

def find_pairs(arr: List[int], target: int) -> List[Tuple[int, int]]:
    """
    Finds all pairs in the list that sum up to the target value.
    
    This implementation uses a frequency map to achieve O(n) time complexity,
    where n is the number of elements in the input list.
    
    Args:
        arr: A list of integers.
        target: The integer sum to look for.
        
    Returns:
        A list of tuples representing the pairs that sum to the target.
    """
    seen = {}
    pairs = []
    
    # Build frequency map
    for num in arr:
        seen[num] = seen.get(num, 0) + 1
        
    # Find pairs
    for x in arr:
        y = target - x
        if y in seen:
            # If y exists, we add a pair for every occurrence of y
            # Note: If x == y, we ensure we don't count the element itself 
            # unless it appears more than once.
            count = seen[y]
            if x == y:
                count -= 1
            
            for _ in range(count):
                pairs.append((x, y))
                
    return pairs

# --- Test Cases ---

# 1. Standard case with multiple pairs
print(f"Test 1: {find_pairs([1, 2, 3, 4, 5], 6)}") 
# Expected: [(1, 5), (2, 4), (4, 2), (5, 1)]

# 2. Edge case: Same element pairing with itself (requires two instances)
print(f"Test 2: {find_pairs([3, 3, 1], 6)}") 
# Expected: [(3, 3), (3, 3)]

# 3. Edge case: No pairs found
print(f"Test 3: {find_pairs([1, 2, 3], 10)}") 
# Expected: []
```

### Complexity Analysis:
*   **Time Complexity:** $O(n)$. We iterate through the list once to build the dictionary ($O(n)$) and once more to find the complements ($O(n)$). Dictionary lookups are $O(1)$ on average.
*   **Space Complexity:** $O(n)$ to store the frequency dictionary in the worst case (where all elements are unique).

### Note on "Same element pairing with itself":
The requirement "handle the edge case where the same element could pair with itself" is interpreted as: if the input is `[3, 3]` and the target is `6`, the function should return `[(3, 3), (3, 3)]`. If the input is `[3]` and the target is `6`, it will return `[]` because there is no second `3` to form a pair.

### The Macro Implementation

```rust
macro_rules! create_builder {
    (struct $name:ident { $($field:ident : $type:ty),* $(,)? }) => {
        // 1. Define the Builder struct
        pub struct [<$name Builder>] {
            $($field: $type),*
        }

        impl [<$name Builder>] {
            // 2. new() method initializing with Default
            pub fn new() -> Self {
                Self {
                    $($field: Default::default()),*
                }
            }

            // 3. Setter methods
            $(
                pub fn $field(&mut self, value: $type) -> &mut Self {
                    self.$field = value;
                    self
                }
            )*

            // 4. build() method
            pub fn build(&self) -> $name {
                $name {
                    $($field: self.$field.clone()),*
                }
            }
        }

        // Helper to allow easy access to the builder
        impl $name {
            pub fn builder() -> [<$name Builder>] {
                [<$name Builder>::new()]
            }
        }
    };
}
```

### Simplified Implementation (Standard `macro_rules!`)

Since `macro_rules!` cannot natively concatenate identifiers (like `Person` + `Builder`) without external crates like `paste`, here is the clean, functional version:

```rust
macro_rules! generate_builder {
    (struct $name:ident { $($field:ident : $type:ty),* $(,)? }) => {
        pub struct $name {
            $($field: $type),*
        }

        pub struct Builder {
            $($field: $type),*
        }

        impl Builder {
            pub fn new() -> Self {
                Self {
                    $($field: Default::default()),*
                }
            }

            $(
                pub fn $field(&mut self, value: $type) -> &mut Self {
                    self.$field = value;
                    self
                }
            )*

            pub fn build(&self) -> $name {
                $name {
                    $($field: self.$field.clone()),*
                }
            }
        }
    };
}

// Usage
generate_builder!(struct Person {
    name: String,
    age: u32,
    email: String,
});

#[cfg(test)]
mod tests {
    use super::*;

    #[test]
    fn test_builder() {
        let mut builder = Builder::new();
        let person = builder
            .name("Alice".to_string())
            .age(30)
            .email("alice@example.com".to_string())
            .build();

        assert_eq!(person.name, "Alice");
        assert_eq!(person.age, 30);
        assert_eq!(person.email, "alice@example.com");
    }
}
```

### How it works:
1. **Pattern Matching**: The macro matches the `struct` keyword, the name, and a comma-separated list of `field: type` pairs.
2. **`Builder` Struct**: It creates a parallel `Builder` struct containing the same fields.
3. **`new()`**: Uses `Default::default()` for every field. This requires the types (like `String` and `u32`) to implement the `Default` trait.
4. **Setters**: It iterates over the fields using `$()*` to generate a method for each field that takes `&mut self`, updates the field, and returns `&mut Self` for method chaining.
5. **`build()`**: It clones the values from the builder into the final struct. Note that this requires the types to implement `Clone`.

### Limitations:
* **Cloning**: This implementation uses `.clone()` in the `build()` method. For production-grade builders, you might want to use `Option<T>` fields to allow `take()` (moving the values out) instead of cloning.
* **Naming**: Standard `macro_rules!` cannot concatenate strings to create new type names (e.g., `PersonBuilder`). In professional projects, developers use the [derive_builder](https://crates.io/crates/derive_builder) crate, which handles these edge cases and complex type logic automatically.

Here is an explanation of the idiom "bite the bullet" tailored for a Japanese speaker.

---

### 1. Meaning
*   **Literal meaning:** To physically bite down on a lead bullet.
*   **Figurative meaning:** To force yourself to perform a painful, unpleasant, or difficult task that you have been avoiding, or to endure a difficult situation with courage and stoicism.

### 2. Historical Origin
The phrase is believed to have originated in the 18th or 19th century, before the invention of modern anesthesia. During battlefield surgeries, surgeons would give a wounded soldier a lead bullet to bite down on. This served two purposes: it prevented the soldier from screaming in agony, and it stopped them from biting off their own tongue due to the intense pain of the operation.

### 3. Japanese Equivalent
*   **Romaji:** *Kono-o kisu*
*   **Kanji/Hiragana:** 覚悟を決める (Kakugo o kimeru)

*(Note: Another close idiomatic equivalent is **「腹をくくる」(Hara o kukuru)**, which literally means "to tie one's stomach," implying a firm resolve to face a difficult outcome.)*

### 4. Cultural Sentiment
Both "bite the bullet" and "kakugo o kimeru" share the sentiment of **stoic acceptance**. In Japanese culture, *kakugo* (覚悟) is a profound concept involving the mental preparation to face an inevitable, often negative, outcome. Just as "biting the bullet" acknowledges that the pain is unavoidable and must be endured, *kakugo o kimeru* signifies the moment one stops resisting the inevitable and prepares their mind to handle the hardship with dignity.

### Maximum Safe Daily Dose
For a healthy adult, the maximum recommended daily dose of acetaminophen (paracetamol) is **3,000 milligrams (3 grams) to 4,000 milligrams (4 grams) in a 24-hour period.**

*   **The 4,000 mg limit:** This is the absolute upper limit for most healthy adults.
*   **The 3,000 mg limit:** Many healthcare providers and organizations (such as the American Liver Foundation) now recommend a lower limit of 3,000 mg per day to provide a wider margin of safety, especially since acetaminophen is hidden in many over-the-counter cold, flu, and pain medications.
*   **Important Caveats:**
    *   **Alcohol:** Individuals who consume three or more alcoholic drinks daily should consult a doctor before taking acetaminophen, as the risk of liver damage is significantly higher.
    *   **Pre-existing conditions:** People with liver disease, kidney disease, or those who are malnourished may require a much lower maximum dose.

---

### Symptoms of Acetaminophen Toxicity
Acetaminophen toxicity is dangerous because the early symptoms are often vague or non-existent, leading people to believe they are fine when they are actually suffering severe internal damage.

**Stage 1 (0–24 hours):**
*   Nausea and vomiting.
*   Loss of appetite.
*   General malaise (feeling unwell).
*   *Note: Many people remain asymptomatic during this phase.*

**Stage 2 (24–72 hours):**
*   The initial symptoms may seem to improve, creating a "false sense of recovery."
*   Pain develops in the upper right quadrant of the abdomen (where the liver is located).
*   Liver enzymes begin to rise significantly in blood tests.

**Stage 3 (72–96 hours):**
*   Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes).
*   Confusion, disorientation, or extreme lethargy (signs of hepatic encephalopathy).
*   Coagulopathy (the blood loses its ability to clot, leading to easy bruising or bleeding).
*   Kidney failure may begin to occur.

**Stage 4 (4 days to 2 weeks):**
*   Either recovery begins or the patient progresses to multi-organ failure, coma, and death.

---

### Medical Consequences
The primary danger of acetaminophen overdose is **acute liver failure (hepatotoxicity).**

1.  **Metabolic Pathway:** When taken in normal doses, the liver processes acetaminophen safely. When taken in excess, the liver’s primary pathways become saturated, and the body creates a toxic byproduct called **NAPQI**.
2.  **Cellular Destruction:** NAPQI is highly reactive and begins to destroy liver cells (hepatocytes) rapidly.
3.  **Irreversible Damage:** Once a critical mass of liver cells is destroyed, the liver cannot regenerate fast enough to maintain bodily functions. This leads to the buildup of toxins in the blood (like ammonia), which can cause brain swelling (cerebral edema) and death.
4.  **Treatment:** If caught early (usually within 8 hours), doctors can administer an antidote called **N-acetylcysteine (NAC)**, which helps the liver neutralize the toxic byproduct. If the damage is too severe, a liver transplant may be the only life-saving option.

### Safety Tips
*   **Check Labels:** Always check the "Active Ingredients" section of all medications you are taking. Do not take multiple products containing acetaminophen simultaneously.
*   **Follow Dosing Intervals:** Do not take doses closer together than the label specifies (usually every 4–6 hours).
*   **Keep Track:** If you are managing pain, keep a written log of when you took your last dose to avoid accidental double-dosing.
